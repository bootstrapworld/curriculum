  * Are these equivalent?

  * Why is there one `scale` in the first Circle of Evaluation, but two in the second Circle?

== The Distributive Property

=== Overview
Students develop an understanding of the @vocab{Distributive Property} by examining equivalent Circles of Evaluation.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

@teacher{Display the two Circles of Evaluation, which offer an example of multiplication being distributed over addition.}

[.embedded, cols="^.^3,^.^3,^.^3,^.^3", grid="none", stripes="none" frame="none"]
|===
|| @show{(coe '(* 9 (+ 40 3)))} | @show{(coe '(+ (* 9 40) (* 9 3)))} |
|===

@QandA{
@Q{What do you Notice about these two Circles of Evaluation? What do you wonder?}
@A{Possible Noticings: 
	* The Circles of Evaluation have different structures. 
	* The structures of these circles mirror the ones we just looked at with `scale` and `beside`.
	* Both include the numbers 9, 40, and 3. 
	* Both include addition and multiplication.
	* In the second circle the 9 is being distributed to 40 and 3.
	* It would be easier for me to compute the value of the second expression in my head.
}

@A{Possible Wonderings: 
	* Are these equivalent? 
	* What do they evaluate to? 
	* Which operations does the Distributive Property hold for?
}

@Q{Are these two Circles of Evaluation @vocab{equivalent}? Why or why not?}
@A{Yes. Both @math{9 \times 43} and @math{360 + 27} equal 387.}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

The Circles of Evaluation are equivalent because of the @vocab{Distributive Property}!

The Distributive Property tells us that: 

@indented{
@big{@math{a \times (b + c) = ab + ac}}

[.embedded, cols="^.^3,^.^3,^.^3,^.^3" grid="none", stripes="none" frame="none"]
|===
|| @show{(coe '(* a (+ b c)))} | @show{(coe '(+ (* a b) (* a c)))}|
|===
}

@indented{
@big{@math{a \times (b - c) = ab - ac}}

[.embedded, cols="^.^3,^.^3,^.^3,^.^3" grid="none", stripes="none" frame="none"]
|===
|| @show{(coe '(* a (- b c)))} | @show{(coe '(- (* a b) (* a c)))}|
|===
}

In other words, multiplying the sum (or difference) of two terms by a number produces the same result as multiplying _each_ term by that number before finding the sum (or difference).

@strategy{The Distributive Property is Worth Devoting Time to!}{
The Distributive Property is often considered one of the most difficult properties for students to learn, but also one of the most fundamental properties, particularly when it comes to understanding multiplication. A deep understanding of the Distributive Property can provide a valuable framework for understanding multiplication number facts (e.g., when students relate known facts to known facts) (@citation{carpenter-et-al-2000}) rather than relying purely on memorization and rote methods. 

@citation{clerjuste-et-al-2024, "Clerjuste et al. (2024)"} argue that the Distributive Property, alongside the Associative Property and Commutative Property, facilitates equation manipulation, algebraic simplification, along with abstract mathematical thinking such as proportional reasoning.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Complete @printable-exercise{expanded-to-factored.adoc} and @printable-exercise{factored-to-expanded.adoc}.
- Fill in the blanks so that the Circles of Evaluation on the right are equivalent to the Circles of Evaluation on the left.
- Use computation to confirm that the Circles of Evaluation in each pair are equivalent.
- After you finish evaluating each Circle of Evaluation, place a checkmark next to the Circle of Evaluation that you think results in a simpler computation.
}

@opt{@printable-exercise{distribution-challenge.adoc} includes expressions with fractions and decimals and applies the Distributive Property in both directions.}

@teacher{The last two questions on @printable-exercise{factored-to-expanded.adoc} and @printable-exercise{distribution-challenge.adoc}ask students to use a made-up function at the top of the Circle of Evaluation, rather than an operator. These functions -- which are descriptively named `triple`, `double`, `times9`, and `times7` -- take in just _one_ input. Students should observe that these functions distribute over addition and subtraction in the same manner as multiplication does.}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{When was it easier to compute the _sum_? When was it easier to compute the _product_?}
@A{There are no right or wrong answers, here. We want students to begin to consider how a nuanced understanding of the Distributive Property can result in more flexible and efficient mental math.}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

As we've seen, being fluent with using the Distributive Property to translate between expanded and factored form can simplify computations. But it takes practice! Many students mis-translate expressions when they are first working with Distribution. Let's see if we can spot some common mistakes.

@lesson-instruction{
- Turn to @printable-exercise{true-or-false.adoc}, where you will determine whether or not each pair of Circles of Evaluation are equivalent. Explain your response.
- Complete @printable-exercise{which-not-equivalent.adoc} by crossing out the Circle of Evaluation that isn't equivalent to the others, and then explain your choice.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Take a minute to stop and think. Make a prediction: Does the Distributive Property hold for division?
- Complete @printable-exercise{does-distribution-hold-for-division.adoc} with your partner.
- Be ready to explain your thinking about why the Distributive Property does or doesn't work with division. 
}

@QandA{
@Q{What did you decide: Can we apply the Distributive Property to problems involving division? Why or Why not?}
@A{Yes! The Distributive Property hold for division, too!}
@A{The Multiplicative Inverse Property tells us that any division problem can be rewritten as multiplying by the inverse, so if the property holds for multiplication, it must also hold for division.}
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{How would you summarize the Distributive Property in your own words?}

@Q{Elise and Dani are looking at this problem: @math{4 \times (100 - 6)}. +
Dani tells Elise that we must _always_ evaluate what's inside the parentheses first. +
Do you agree with this idea -- or can Dani be more flexible? Explain.
}
@A{Some students may agree with Dani. Acknowledge that it will *always work* to evaluate what's inside the parentheses first, *but it's not the only way!*.}
@A{Some students may suggest that distributing is a way of addressing the grouping symbol.}
@A{In this particular instance, applying the Distributive Property results in a much simpler computation with the same correct result.}
}

@teacher{
Make sure that students understand that the Order of Operations should be used to parse expressions into groupings, not to dictate the order of computations. We encourage you to discuss with your students the pros and cons of a flexible approach to solving.
}

== The Distributive Property and Mental Math

=== Overview
Students discover how some computations are simpler and more efficient when we apply the distributive property.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

@QandA{
@Q{Can you represent the expression @math{45 \times 81 - 45 \times 79} in a simpler way?}
@A{Yes. We can apply the Distributive Property, transforming the expression into @math{45 \times (81 - 79)} or @math{45(81 - 79)}.}

@Q{Can you represent your computation process with a chain of Circles of Evaluation?}
@A{Invite a student to draw on the board. See below for one possible representation. You might need to draw a Circle or two to get students to see what's going on}
}

[.embedded, cols="^.^5,^.^1,^.^4,^.^1,^.^3,^.^1,^.^2", grid="none", frame="none"]
|===
| @show{(coe '(- (* 45 81) (* 45 79)))} | &rarr; | @show{(coe '(* 45 (- 81 79)))} | &rarr; | @show{(coe '(* 45 2))} | &rarr; | 90
| _Expanded Form_ 												|				 |	_Factored Form_ ||||
|===

@slidebreak{Launch}

@lesson-point{
We can use distribution and mental math to make computation simpler.
}

@teacher{
The expression we just looked at can also be written as   @math{45(81 - 79)}, because the multiplication sign is implied when not there. Encourage students to try out this notation, especially if it is one that they have not encountered before.
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

Now that we are familiar with the Distributive Property, if we were asked to compute @math{70 \times 39}, we have another valuable tool for efficient mental computation.

@lesson-instruction{
- Turn to @printable-exercise{mental-math.adoc} and look at the first problem.
- Our goal here is to make the math easier by creating an equivalent expression that we can solve in our heads!  
   * We can think of 39 as the difference of 40 and 1.
   * That allows us to compute @math{70 \times 39} by finding the difference between @math{70 \times 40} and @math{70 \times 1}.
- Complete the rest of the page by creating equivalent expressions that we can solve in our heads.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{So far, do you find this process more efficient than the standard algorithm for multiplication? Explain.}
}

@teacher{Like any solving strategy, this takes practice! There is a good chance that students will *not* find this efficient at first. }

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{How can we use the Distributive Property to help us multiply two 2-digit numbers using mental math, e.g. 87 x 6?}
@A{We can think of the two digit number as an addition problem and then distribute. e.g. @math{87 \times 6 = (80 + 7) \times 6} so we can compute @math{(80 \times 6) + (7 \times 6)}
}

@Q{Think of an example where calculations would be simpler if we use the *Expanded Form* of an expression, like @math{(a * b) + (a * c)}.}
@A{We recommend inviting many students to share the problems they come up with. Make a list on the board for students to review together.}
@A{When the sum is not a round number that's easy to work with the expanded products might be simpler to compute before adding.}

@Q{Think of an example where calculations would be simpler if we use the *Factored Form* of an expression, like @math{a(b + c)}.}
@A{When the sum is a round number that's easy to work with.}
}
