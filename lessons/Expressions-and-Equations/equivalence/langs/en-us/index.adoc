== Programming Exploration: Are They Equivalent?

=== Overview
Students consider what makes one line of code produce output that is equivalent to the output of another line of code.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

What if a programmer wants to determine whether two lines of code will produce the same result? They have two options. They can test the code and see what happens. More experienced programmers, however, study the structure of the code. They think about the functions being used... and then come to a conclusion. These programmers are more strategic and efficient when they write their own code.

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}
We are going to consider whether two lines of image-producing code produce perfectly identical images. If so, those lines of code are @vocab{equivalent}.

@lesson-instruction{
- Turn to @printable-exercise{true-or-false-images.adoc}.
- Discuss each pair of image-producing expressions with your partner before predicting whether the images will be identical or not.
- Test the code in the @starter-file{equivalence}, then explain why the images are or aren't equivalent.
}

@teacher{As students work, encourage them to _always_ make predictions before testing the code. Similarly, the activity will be more valuable if students discuss _why_ the code did or did not produce identical images. Debrief to ensure comprehension.}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{What were some of the strategies you used to predict whether or not lines of code were equivalent?}
@A{Responses will vary. Sample responses might include:}
@A{We paid attention to the order of arguments}
@A{We referred to contracts to understand the effect of each input.}
@A{We used computation.}
}

@teacher{Ensuring comprehension of @printable-exercise{true-or-false-images.adoc} will lead to a smoother experience on @printable-exercise{writing-equivalent-code.adoc}. In the second activity, just _one_ line of code is provided -- and students develop the second on their own. Students will exercise a different cognitive muscle here: rather than making a prediction about code, they must write their own!}

@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}

@lesson-instruction{
- Turn to @printable-exercise{writing-equivalent-code.adoc}. 
- With your partner, write a _different_ but equivalent line of code. If you get stuck, refer to your contracts and draw Circles of Evaluation!
- Test the pairs of code in @starter-file{equivalence} to make sure they produce identical images.
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{What does it mean for two expressions to be equivalent?}
@A{They produce the same output.}
}

== Computation and Equivalence

=== Overview
Students explore a simple @vocab{arithmetic expression} to create as many equivalent Circles of Evaluation as they can. They then categorize the Circles of Evaluation they've generated to consider some of the different ways in which expressions can be equivalent.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{LaunchR}

Let's look at this @vocab{Circle of Evaluation}:

@show{(coe '(+ 10 6))}

@slidebreak{LaunchR}

To create a Circle of Evaluation of the same value, we could write this:

@show{(coe '(+ (+ 8 2) 6))}

Because we knew that @math{8 + 2} produces 10, we could use it _in place of 10_.

In fact, we could replace 10 with a variety of different expressions to produce an @vocab{equivalent} Circle of Evaluation -- one which simplifies to the same value.

We could also simply write @math{16}!

@slidebreak{Launch}

In this last example, the Circle of Evaluation has been simplified down to just one number; since every Circle of Evaluation must have an operator or function, the Circle disappears.

@lesson-point{
Numeric expressions are @vocab{equivalent} when they simplify to the same value.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

@QandA{
@Q{Can you think of other Circles of Evaluation equivalent to @math{16}?}
@A{Possible responses: @math{4  \times 4}; @math{15 + 1}; @math{4 \times 8 \div 2}}
}

@lesson-instruction{
- On a piece of paper, you and your partner are going to create as many equivalent Circles of Evaluation for @math{16} as you can!
- You can use any operators you want and as many as you want, but only the numbers 2, 4, and 8.
- Compute the value of each Circle of Evaluation to confirm equivalence.
- Think you've made every possible Circle of Evaluation given those constraints? +
Challenge yourself to make other Circles of Evaluation that evaluate to 16 and:
 * include the number 1000 
 * include the fraction @math{\frac{1}{2}}
 * use as many nested Circles as you possibly can
 * only use odd numbers
}

@teacher{
This activity offers students an opportunity to practice constructing Circles of Evaluation as they explore the notion of equivalence.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

Circles of Evaluation help us to see the underlying structure of mathematics. They visually highlight the way in which expressions with the same value can have a variety of unique mathematical structures.

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Let's collect all of the Circles of Evaluation you drew using @math{2, 4} and @math{8} on the board. Please add them one at a time, making sure not to repeat someone else's contribution. 
  * To clarify, @math{10 + 6}, for instance, is not exactly the same as @math{6 + 10} -- so, if we were including circles with @math{6} and @math{10}, we would want to see both @show{(coe '(+ 6 10))} and @show{(coe '(+ 10 6))} on the board.
- Once we have all of your ideas recorded on the board, we'll take a look.
}

@QandA{
@Q{What do you Notice?}
@Q{What do you Wonder?}
}

@strategy{Pedagogy Note: Notice and Wonder!}{
This pedagogy is a @link{https://sadlerscience.com/notice-and-wonder/, widely-used best practice in Math-Ed}, and is used throughout this course. In the "Notice" phase, students are asked to crowd-source their observations. No observation is too small or too silly! By listening to other students' observations, students may find themselves taking a closer look. The "Wonder" phase involves students raising questions, but they must also explain the context for those questions. Sharon Hessney (moderator for the NYTimes excellent @link{https://www.NYtimes.com/column/whats-going-on-in-this-graph, What's Going On in This Graph?} activity) sometimes calls this "what do you wonder...and *why*?" Both of these phases should be done in groups or as a whole class, with adequate time given to each.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@teacher{After students have shared, explain the next element of the activity.}

@lesson-instruction{
- Let’s identify some possible groupings for these Circles of Evaluation..
- There is no right or wrong way to group them. Groupings could include any number of cards. You may choose to put one Circle of Evaluation into multiple groups, and you may leave some Circles of Evaluation ungrouped.
- You and your partner will record your groupings on a piece of paper. Come up with a name for each group so that someone else could identify what you think makes that group unique.
- Who sees a possible grouping for these Circles of Evaluation?
}

@teacher{
Work with students to identify one or two groupings as a class. If students are struggling to sort the Circles of Evaluation, you might offer one or more examples.}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{Let's share out the category names we developed to make a class list.}
@A{Some example groupings:
  * I'm putting @math{10 + 6} and @math{6 + 10} into a group called "10 and 6" because they both include 10 and 6.
  * I’m going to put @math{10 + 6}, @math{6 + 10}, and @math{7 - 4} into a group called "Single Circles" because they are both examples of expressions with exactly one Circle (no nesting).
  * I'm putting @math{10 + 6} and @math{20 - 4} into a group called "Sixteens" because they both evaluate to 16.
}
}

@teacher{Have pairs share out their category names. There is no need to evaluate categories’ names or qualify students’ observations; rather, challenge students to identify and articulate patterns they have observed to lay a foundation upon which to formalize the laws of arithmetic.}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{What do you Notice and Wonder about the groupings we just made?}
@Q{Are there any groupings of expressions that are mirror-images of one another?}
@Q{Are there any groupings that all compute the same answer?}
@Q{Are there any groupings that have the same numbers and operations, but shuffled into different orders?}
}

@teacher{
Subsequent lessons in this series introduce students to the Associative Property, the Commutative Property, the Identity Property, and the Distributive Property. Although students may not know these properties by name, some conversation about the properties will likely arise organically throughout the activity. There is no need to name properties today; instead, highlight students’ contributions and probe.

For instance, if a student observes that @math{2 \times 8} is the same as @math{8 \times 2}, you might inquire whether the equivalence holds if we use division rather than multiplication.

In short: there are no wrong answers here! The goal is to help students develop a deep yet flexible understanding of the language of mathematics.
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{What does it mean for two expressions to be equivalent?}
@A{They evaluate to the same thing.}
}


== Simplifying Arithmetic Expressions

=== Overview
Students use Circles of Evaluation to simplify arithmetic expressions to a single value.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

@teacher{Because Circles of Evaluation help students visualize the structure of the math, they are a terrific solving tool. They create structure for students while simultaneously offering more flexibility than adhering to a strict sequential solving algorithm.}

[.embedded, cols="^.^2,^.^3,^.^1,^.^3,^.^1,^.^3,^.^2", grid="none", stripes="none", frame="none"]
|===
|| @show{(coe '(+ 3 (- 14 5)))} | &rarr; | @show{(coe '(+ 3 9))} | &rarr; | @math{12} |
|===

@QandA{
@Q{Why is the first Circle of Evaluation (above) equivalent to the second Circle of Evaluation? Why is the second Circle of Evaluation equivalent to the final result?}
@A{To get from the the first Circle of Evaluation to the next: @math{5} less than @math{14} becomes 9. To get to the final result, @math{3} increased by @math{9} becomes @math{12}.}}

@slidebreak{Launch}

[.embedded, cols="^.^2,^.^4,^.^1,^.^3,^.^1,^.^3,^.^2", grid="none", stripes="none", frame="none"]
|===
|| @show{(coe '(+ (- 10 8) (* 3 6)))} | &rarr; | @show{(coe '(+ 2 18))} | &rarr; | @math{20}|
|===

@QandA{
@Q{Does the order in which we evaluate the two inner Circles (above) matter?  Why or why not?}
@A{No, the order does not matter! We could evaluate the Circle on the left first, or the Circle on the right first because the Circles are independent of one another.}
@A{We do have to evaluate both of the inner circles before we can find their sum!}
}

@strategy{Pedagogy Note: A Flexible Order of Operations?}{
_Think for a moment about a commonly heard statement in teaching the Order of Operations: “You work from left to right.” At another point in the curriculum, when working on properties of the operations, we say, “You can add numbers in any order” (commutative property). How can both of these statements be true? Preparing students to *do mathematics* means that they have an integrated understanding of rules and properties in mathematics._

@indented{From Order of Operations: The myth and the math (@citation{bay-williams-martinie-2015})}

To recap: *yes*, we are advocating for flexible thinking about the Order of Operations that relies on students' abilities to make sense of the underlying structure of math before tackling computation!
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Each row on @printable-exercise{computation-whole-nums.adoc} represents a step-by-step computation, which results in an answer. Some of the steps are missing numbers and operators!
- Fill in those numbers and operators so that each sequence of Circles will end with the answer shown on the right.
- When you're done, complete @printable-exercise{computation-frac-dec.adoc}, a version of the activity with more challenging numbers.
- Did you fill in blanks in the Circles of Evaluation from left to right or right to left? Why?
}

@teacher{
For additional practice with this skill, you might have your students attempt @opt-printable-exercise{computation-whole-nums-2.adoc} (with simpler numbers and computations) or @opt-printable-exercise{computation-whole-nums-3.adoc}.}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{When we look at a Circle of Evaluation, what is a clue we could look for that might tell us that we have to do one computation before another?}
@A{If a circle is inside of another circle, we have to complete the inner computation before we complete the outer computation.}
@A{If two circles are not inside of each other, we can compute either one before computing the other.}
}

== Are They Equivalent?

=== Overview
Students explore computation and equivalence through two different activities -- "True or False?" and "Which One Doesn't Belong?"

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

@strategy{Pedagogy Note: Viewing the Equal Sign as Relational}{
Students often interpret the equal sign _operationally_, or they think of it as an instruction that means "now get the answer." Students with an operational view of the equal sign often solve solve 8 + 4 = ? + 5 incorrectly, as either 12 or 17 (@citation{falkner-et-al-1999}).

Conversely, students who have a relational view of the equal sign recognize that a relationship exists between the numbers or expressions on either side of the equal sign. Decades of research @citation{knuth-et-al-2006, "(Knuth et al., 2006, for example)"} suggest that students who interpret the equal sign to mean "the same as" are better positioned to think algebraically down the road.

These activities are designed to help students develop a _relational view_ of the equal sign. The "True or False?" activity is based on research by @citation{carpenter-et-al-2003, "Carpenter et al (2003)"}, who recommend introducing number sentences that encourage students to examine their conceptions of the meaning of the equal sign.
}

@lesson-instruction{
- Turn to @printable-exercise{true-or-false.adoc}.
- With your partner, consider each pair of expressions on the page.
- Decide whether or note they are equivalent and explain how you know.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

@QandA{
@Q{What strategies did you use to decide whether or not the expressions were equivalent?}

@Q{Were there any pairs you weren't sure about?}
}

@teacher{Confirm that students agree on which pairs were and weren't equivalent.}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

@teacher{"Which One Doesn't Belong," has students analyze four different Circles of Evaluation to determine which one is not equivalent. Model your thought process before inviting students to work.}

@lesson-instruction{
Complete @printable-exercise{which-not-equivalent.adoc}, looking closely at each set of Circles of Evaluation to determine the one that is not equivalent.
}

@strategy{Pedagogy Note: Which One Doesn't Belong?}{
There are numerous benefits to inviting students to search out similarities and difference in a group of items (in this case, Circles of Evaluation). In articulating the differences that they notice between Circles of Evaluation, students will likely tune into to details that they might have otherwise overlooked.

If your students are getting stuck, start a conversation! What do students notice, at first glance? What makes the Circles of Evaluation alike, and what makes them different? Have students share their reasoning to create opportunities for peer learning.
}

@QandA{
@Q{What strategies did you use to determine whether or not Circles of Evaluation were equivalent?}
@Q{Did you find that some strategies were more efficient than others? Why?}
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{Can two expressions be equivalent even if their structure is different?}
@A{Yes. As long as they evaluate to the same thing.}

@Q{Is it possible to be sure that two expressions will be equivalent without knowing that they evaluate to the same thing?}
@A{Yes. Sometimes understanding the relationship between the structure of two expressions is enough to know that they are equivalent.}
}
