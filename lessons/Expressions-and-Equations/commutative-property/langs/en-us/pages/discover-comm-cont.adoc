@n Now look at two more Circles of Evaluation to see how the Commutative Property holds for problems involving *addition of three values*. Can you fill in the remaining Circles of Evaluation so that the _order_ changes, but not the _groupings_?

[.FillVerticalSpace, cols="^.^1,^.^1,^.^1,^.^"]
|===
| @show{(coe '(+ (+ 9 2) 8))}
| @show{(coe  '(+ 8 (+ 9 2)))}
| @show{(coe  '(+ (+ (?ANS 2) (?ANS 9)) 8))}
| @show{(coe  '(+ 8 (+ (?ANS 2) (?ANS 9))))}
| @math{(9 + 2) + 8 = } @fitb{5em}{@ifsoln{@math{ 19}}}
| @math{8 + (9 + 2) = } @fitb{5em}{@ifsoln{@math{ 19}}}
| @ifsoln{@math{(2 + 9) + 8 = 19}}
| @ifsoln{@math{(2 + 9) + 8 = 19}}
|===

What do you notice? @fitb{}{}

@blanklines{2}{@ifsoln{All of the expressions evaluate to 19. In the first expression, we add 9 and 2 first, while in the second expression, we have moved the position of 9 + 2, so that 11 is added to 8.}}

@vspace{1ex}

These Circles of Evaluation _all_ represent the *Commutative Property of Addition*! Notice how, when we used three values, there were multiple ways of reconfiguring the numbers. 

@n Let's see whether the Commutative Property of Multiplication also holds for problems involving *multiplication of three values*. Can you fill in the remaining Circles of Evaluation so that the _order_ changes, but not the _groupings_?

[.FillVerticalSpace, cols="^.^1,^.^1,^.^1,^.^"]
|===
| @show{(coe '(* (* 11 2) 3))}
| @show{(coe  '(* 3 (* 11 2)))}
| @show{(coe  '(* (* (?ANS 2) (?ANS 11)) 3))}
| @show{(coe  '(* 3 (* (?ANS 2) (?ANS 11))))}
| @math{(11 \times 2) \times 3 = } @fitb{5em}{@ifsoln{66}}
| @math{3 \times (11 \times 2) = } @fitb{5em}{@ifsoln{66}}
| @ifsoln{@math{(2 \times 11) \times 3 = 66}}
| @ifsoln{@math{3 \times (11 \times 2) = 66}}
|===

What do you notice? @fitb{}{}

@blanklines{2}{@ifsoln{All of the expressions evaluate to 66.}}
