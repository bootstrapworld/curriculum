Decide whether or not the two expressions are equivalent. Explain your reasoning.

[.FillVerticalSpace, cols="^.^1a, ^.^5a, ^.^1a, ^.^5a, ^.^10a", grid="none", options="header", ]
|===
|	 | Circle 1 							| 	| Circle 2								
| True or False? Justify.

| @n |@show{(coe '(/ (+ a b) f))}			| = |  @show{(coe '(/ f (+ b a)))} 
| @ifsoln{False. The Commutative Property does not hold for division.}

| @n |@show{(coe '(+ w (* n t)))}			| = |  @show{(coe '(+ (* n t) w))}   
| @ifsoln{True. The Commutative Property indicates that @math{n \times t} is the same as @math{t \times n}.}

| @n |@show{(coe '(* (* q g) h))}			| = |  @show{(coe '(* (* g q) h))}  
| @ifsoln{True. We can change the order and grouping of three values being multiplied.}

| @n |@show{(coe '(* (* 2 2) c))}			| = |  @show{(coe '(* c 4))}   
| @ifsoln{True. We can use computation to replace @math{2 \times 2} with 4. Commutativity indicates that we can change the order of the factors as well.}

| @n |@show{(coe '(* v (- n 6)))}			| = |  @show{(coe '(* (- 6 n) v))}
| @ifsoln{False. Subtraction is not commutative.}

| @n |@show{(coe '(/ (+ y x) 5))}			| = |  @show{(coe '(/ (+ x y) 5))} 
| @ifsoln{True. The Commutative Property indicates that we can change the order of two addends.}
