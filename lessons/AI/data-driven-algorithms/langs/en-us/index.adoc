== Spell-checking: The Algorithm @duration{15 minutes}
@objective{ai-machines-v-people}

=== Overview
Students consider one specific example of a data driven algorithm: evaluating how spell checkers' programmed behaviors compare and contrast with the behaviors of humans.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

@lesson-instruction{
Consider this sentence with your partner:

@big{Teh cat meowed so loudky it woke thm upp.}

- What do you think the author intended to write?
- Be prepared to describe how you did your spell checking:
  * How do you locate misspelled words?
  * How do you correct misspelled words?
  * How would you describe each of the spelling mistakes that the author made?
}

@slidebreak{Launch}
@QandA{
@Q{How did you find the misspelled words?}
@A{Answers will vary, but most people would say "I just knew it when I saw it". This is important to call out!}

@Q{When you found a misspelled word, how did you figure out how to correct it?}
@A{Possible responses might include things like:
  * There was only one word it could be!
  * I could tell from the rest of the sentence.
  * I've made this same typing mistake a million times.
  }

@Q{The author made four different kinds of mistakes when writing this sentence. What were they?}
@A{*Teh*   Reversed the letters `h` and `e`}
@A{*loudky*   Typed `k` instead of `l`}
@A{*thm*   Left out the letter `e`}
@A{*upp*   Added an extra `p` at the end}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}
When humans spell check, they rely on _context clues, experience, and background_. The process of spell-checking varies not just by person, but by situation. If a complex, technical word had been misspelled, for example, you might have relied on a different spell checking strategy. 

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

The spell checker built into your web browser is a classic example of @vocab{machine learning}. To get a better sense of how machines work differently from us, we are going to follow the algorithm from one of the earliest forms of AI/ML.

@lesson-instruction{
- To complete @printable-exercise{first-spell-checker.adoc}, _you_ will be the computer, following an early spell checker's algorithm.
- Reflect on the activity and how it compares to your own strategies.
}

@teacher{
After students have completed the activity, invite them to share their responses to the three reflection questions (below) in small groups.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{What do you think are some limitations of the first spell checker's algorithm?}
@A{There is an extremely limited number of ways in which a word can be misspelled. For example, if two separate letters in a word are incorrect, the spell checker has no chance of proposing the correct word!}

@Q{How is the first spell checker's algorithm similar to whatever strategy *you* use when spell checking your writing?}
@A{See what your students propose, and discuss. If they come up with any similarities, we'd love to hear what they are!}

@Q{How is the first spell checker's algorithm different from whatever strategy *you* use when spell checking your writing?}
@A{When you check spelling, you rely a lot on context, instinct, and previously acquired knowledge.}
@A{The first spell checking program did not have, or use, any of these strengths.}
@A{In contrast, however, a human would not have the time, ability, or concentration to systematically list out thousands of potential words, whereas the computer has no difficulty doing so.}
@A{Therefore, the strategies used by the human and computer may be extremely different for a similar task.}
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{In what ways do spell checkers succeed at mimicking humans?}
@A{They don't! But the outcomes might be similar, depending on a wide variety of factors.}

@Q{Would it be a good idea for a human to try mimic a spell checker's algorithm? Why or why not?}
@A{No. it would be time-consuming, laborious, and highly error prone for a human.}
@A{The program we just discussed relies on computational brute force: primitive spell checking programs will repeatedly edit / transform a misspelled word, generating dozens of different words to verify in the dictionary.}
}


== Spell Checking in Pyret @duration{25 minutes}
@objective{data-driven-algos}

=== Overview
Students explore both the algorithm and the datasets that power a Pyret-based spell checker, discovering that @vocab{data-driven algorithms} are at the heart of AI.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch-DN}

@lesson-instruction{
- Let's take a look at a spell checking program written in Pyret.
- Open the @starter-file{spell-checker} and click "Run".
- Complete the first section of @printable-exercise{pyret-spell-checker.adoc}.
}

@teacher{Heads up: The functions used in this Pyret starter file only work in this particular starter file.}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{How did the spell checker's work compare to your efforts to be a computer on @printable-exercise{first-spell-checker.adoc}?}
@A{It found everything we did and more than a hundred other insertions and substitutions in mere seconds!}

@Q{Were all of Pyret's suggestions useful?}
@A{No! Most of the words it proposed were nonsense.}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

All of the "words" our spell checker generated in the first section involved a single change. A more technical term with the same meaning is @vocab{edit distance} - the edit distance for all of those words was 1. @vocab{Edit distance} is one way that we can measure the similarity of two strings! 

@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}

@lesson-instruction{
- Let's take a look at how Pyret generates lists of words with a bigger edit distance and refines its suggestions to remove nonsensical words.
- Complete the rest of @printable-exercise{pyret-spell-checker.adoc}.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{What did you discover about the dictionaries in @starter-file{spell-checker}?}

@Q{Why did `alt-words` return a different number of results, depending on which dictionary it was given?}
@A{Because smaller dictionaries didn't know as many words, so `only-real` was removing words even though they could be found in larger dictionaries.}
}

When we fed _more representative data_ to our spell checker, we got better results _without changing the spell checker's code_.

@lesson-point{
The effectiveness of @vocab{data-driven algorithms} is directly tied to how _representative_ the data is.
} 

The same algorithm that almost seems "intelligent" when given 10,000 of the most commonly used words in English could appear _useless_ with a dataset of only 100 words, or the names of 10,000 chemicals.

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{SynthesizeR}

@QandA{
Look at the screenshot (right) of a text messaging app's suggestions for the possibly misspelled word "Cose". The app suggests the original word ("Cose"), indicating that no error was made, along with two alternatives: "Code" and "Close".

@right{@image{images/text-app.png, 250}}

@Q{What sort of _algorithm_ do you think the app used in developing possible alternative words?}
@A{Responses will vary.}
@A{Students may refer to the algorithms discussed in the first half of the lesson.} 
@A{Students might also imagine more complicated algorithms - for instance, algorithms that consider the proximity of letters on the keyboard!}

@Q{What sort of _data_ do you think the spell checking app used in developing possible alternative words?}
@A{Responses will vary.}
@A{Which words the user is most likely to type}
@A{The topic of the text conversation and the most probable next word} 
@A{The dictionary of words the app draws from}
}

@teacher{Students will discuss a similar screenshot of a text messaging app in our lesson on @lesson-link{stat-lang-models-generating-text}. During that lesson, however, students explore how generative AI uses data-driven algorithms to determine what word to produce next.}

== Defining Values
@objective{Define values of various types}
@objective{Simplify a complex expression by replacing repeated parts with defined names,}
@objective{Explain why variables are useful in math and programming}

=== Overview
Students are introduced to variable definition in Pyret, motivated by a desire to save computationally-expensive operations by re-using their results.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch-DN}

@QandA{
Let's experiment with a bigger list of suggestions...

- In the Interactions Window on the right type `alt-words("plation", WORDS-L, 4)` and hit "Enter" to see the table.

@Q{How long did Pyret take to generate all the substitutions, deletions, insertions, and swaps, and then filter by only real words?}
@A{A while!}

@Q{Would asking it to do the _same thing_ over again go faster? Why or why not?}
@A{Responses may vary}

@Q{Try it out! What happened? Can you explain why?}
@A{It still took forever, because we are asking it to redo the same work.}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
When we ask Pyret to do something over and over, it has no way of knowing that it should save its work! As a result, _it does the work over and over, too!_ Fortunately, the world of math has a solution: define a @vocab{variable}.

@QandA{
In the *Interactions Area*, evaluate `results = alt-words("plation", WORDS-L, 4)`.

@Q{Did anything come back? Why or why not?}
@A{No! Defining a variable doesn't produce anything *just like in math!*}

@Q{Now evaluate `results` three times. What happens?}
@A{It was lightning fast!}

@Q{Click "Run", and evaluate `results`. What happens?}
@A{We get an error message! Pyret "forgot" about our variable}

@Q{Evaluate `feeling = "Joy"` and hit Enter. What do you think will happen if we evaluate `feeling`?}
@A{We'll get back `"Joy"`}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
When we want Pyret to remember a definition, we add it to the *Definitions Area*. Every time we click "Run" Pyret re-runs all the code, including the definition!

@lesson-instruction{
- Add `results = alt-words("plation", WORDS-L, 4)` to the Definitions Area, and click "Run". Evaluate `results` in the Interactions Area.
- Add a _second_ definition to the Interactions Area
- What happens if you add another definition that _also_ has the name `results`?
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}
@QandA{
@Q{Why are variable definitions valuable?}
@A{We can save expensive work}

@Q{What kinds of values can we define, besides just tables of spelling corrections?}
@A{Anything!}

@Q{Why can't we define two variables with the same name?}
@A{Pyret only allows a variable name to have one value.}
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}
@QandA{
@Q{Now that we can generate and save spelling suggestions, what are some things we might want to _do_ with them?}
@A{We might want to sort the results, putting the suggestions with shorter edit distances first}
@A{We might want to just show the "top 5", rather than a table with hundreds of entries}
@A{Maybe we could make a chart or a graph?}
}
