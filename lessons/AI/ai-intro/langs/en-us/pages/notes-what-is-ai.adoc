= What Is Artificial Intelligence?

People talk about Artificial Intelligence (AI) as if computers have started to _think_. But AI isn't about machine thinking at all! Underneath, every AI tool is just a computer following an @vocab{algorithm} -- a series of steps that tell a computer how to perform a task.

@vspace{1ex}

== AI Isn't About Machine "Thinking"

Computer scientist John McCarthy described the *AI effect*: as soon as a technology actually works, people stop calling it AI.

- A 1961 spell-checker just compared each word against a dictionary, reporting when a word wasn't in the dictionary. Nobody thought that was "thinking."
- A 1971 spell-checker (SPELL) would _suggest_ corrections, and people swore AI had arrived. _"How does it know what I meant to type?"_ But eventually they got used to it, and stopped calling it AI.
- The same thing happened with predictive text on phones in the 2000s.

Each of these was *just an algorithm* -- the computer didn't know what a word _meant_, or what the writer was _thinking_. Humans keep _moving the goalposts_ for what counts as "intelligence."

@vspace{1ex}

*Machines and people often solve the same task in completely different ways.* When you spell-check, you rely on context, instinct, and experience. A computer can't do any of that -- but it _can_ mechanically generate and check thousands of possible words in seconds. Same task, very different process.

@vspace{1ex}

== AI Is Really About Data-Driven Algorithms

Modern AI doesn't get smarter by "thinking harder" -- it gets better with *better data*.

@vocab{Data-driven algorithms} are algorithms that produce better results when they are given more _representative_ data.

- A spell-checker given a tiny 100-word dictionary gives poor suggestions.
- Give that _same algorithm_ a dictionary of the 10,000 most common English words, and it suddenly seems "smart" -- *with no change to the code.*

@lesson-point{The effectiveness of a data-driven algorithm is directly tied to how _representative_ its data is.}

@vspace{1ex}

== AI Isn't a Monolith -- There Are Different Kinds

Most modern AI is powered by a set of techniques called @vocab{Machine Learning} (ML) -- so much so that people often just say "AI/ML." In Machine Learning, the computer analyzes lots of @vocab{data} and uses statistics to find patterns. Almost every AI tool falls into one of three categories:

@vspace{1ex}

[cols="^1a,^1a,^1a", stripes="none", frame="none", options="header"]
|===
| Prediction | Similarity | Generation

|
"Given an input, predict an output."

@vocab{Classifiers} predict _categories_ (spam or not? which flower?). @vocab{Regression} predicts _values_ (how many points? how long?).

|
"Given an input, find likely matches."

Recommendation systems that find the songs, images, or people most _like_ a given one.

|
"Make something new."

@vocab{Language models} find patterns in language to generate text, images, music, and more.
|===

@vspace{1ex}

The term "Artificial Intelligence" feels a little magical, but "Machine Learning" is very concrete: it's just a collection of *data-driven algorithms* applied to lots of data.
