== What is (and isn't) AI?

=== Overview

Given a number of examples from the real world (image search, self driving cars, etc), students consider which ones are "thinking" vs. "just following steps". The goal of this section is for students to question what "thinking" is -- _not_ to learn about algorithms. However, teachers who want to go deeper into algorithms can do so here.

=== Launch

@QandA{
@Q{Raise your hand if you've ever used some kind of Artificial Intelligence?}
@A{Lots of students raise their hands. ("oh good, we have some experts here!")}
@Q{What are some examples of AI?}
@A{Chatbot LLMs: ChatGPT, Gemini, etc. (Wave these away and dig for more examples!)}
@A{YouTube recommending my next video!}
@A{Self-driving cars!}
@A{Playing a video game against the computer!}
@Q{Is a calculator an example of AI, when it "figures out" how to graph a line? Why or why not?}
@A{No! It's just following instructions or steps.}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

A calculator is definitely not thinking! It's just blindly _following steps_ to plot each point, add numbers, etc. But it's not always easy to tell whether something is blindly following steps, or is truly "thinking!"

When the first calculator came out, people were amazed! They couldn't believe that it could "do math", and were convinced that machines had started thinking for themselves.

@slidebreak{Launch}

Modern AI is largely driven by a set of techniques called @vocab{Machine Learning} (ML for short). In fact, modern AI relies _so deeply_ on Machine Learning that many people use the terms interchangeably!

In @vocab{Machine Learning}, the computer analyzes lots of @vocab{data}, and looks for patterns. But how can something like a _song_ or an _image_ be turned into data?

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Tools like Spotify are often considered AI because they "understand music": we can pick any song, and it finds other songs that _it knows are similar_.

@QandA{
@Q{What's a popular song that most of us know?}
@A{You'll get lots of answers - try to pick a song that is most distinct}
}

@teacher{Open @link{https://open.spotify.com/, Spotify}, search for the song, and play a few seconds of it}

Turn to @printable-exercise{matching-songs-and-images.adoc}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

*I want more songs like this one!* But a computer can't really _hear_ the song the way we do. Computers like to work in terms of _numbers_. In our example, we can compute numbers about how many seconds long it is, or the average _beats per minute_ ("speed").

Each of these numbers represents an @vocab{attribute} of the song, like length or speed.

@QandA{
@Q{How many other @vocab{attributes} could we compute from a song?}
@A{Average volume!}
@A{% of song that has high pitched noises}
@A{Number of words in the lyrics}
@A{Etc...}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@teacher{Click the three dots (`...`) on the song result, and select "Go to song radio". Let students see the other songs it recommended.}

@lesson-instruction{
In your groups, discuss:
- Were these good matches or not?
- In what ways are the matches _similar_ to our original song?
- In what ways are the matches _different_?
- What @vocab{attributes} do you think the computer was looking at, when it found these matches?
}

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}

Tools like Google Image Search allow us to _search-by-image_: we pick an image, and it finds other images that _it knows are similar_.

*I want more pictures like this one!* But a computer can't really _see_ a picture the way we do. Computers work in terms of _numbers_. In our example, we can compute numbers about how many pixels tall it is, or the average _luminescence_ ("brightness").

@QandA{

@A{Average RGB value!}
@A{% vertical or horizontal symmetry}
@A{Etc...}
}

@QandA{
Suppose Spotify only had Country music in its library.
@Q{Would it still be good at making a radio station if you gave it a Country song?}
@A{Probably}
@Q{Would it still be good at making a radio station if you gave it a Reggae song?}
@A{No}
@Q{If you gave it a Reggae song, would it "give up" and choose _random_ Country songs?}
@A{No - it would still try to match the Country songs to the Reggae song, but the matches would be bad.}
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}

Machine Learning can be applied to virtually any kind of document: songs, images, stories, movies, you name it! People like to talk about AI as if it's "magic"; Machine Learning is actually surprisingly simple.

Machine Learning relies on @vocab{data-driven algorithms}. A calculator follows a series of steps to graph a line, and Google Images and Spotify follow a series of steps to suggest images or songs. But with @vocab{data-driven algorithms}, the suggestions are only good once there's a LOT of data in the system.

== Measuring Similarity

@objective{ai-effect}
@objective{ai-vs-ml}

=== Overview

Students learn that one of the most critical concepts in modern AI is "measuring similarity", and that this concept shows up in virtually every example they're familiar with. They discover that the choice of which attributes to measure -- and which measures to prioritize -- can have a large impact on the quality of the AI.

=== Launch

@slidebreak{Launch}

Suppose you're running an animal shelter: you've got dogs, cats, rabbits, etc. You make a spreadsheet to keep track of them, with every animal in a row and columns for their attributes:

[.pyret-table,cols="5a,5a,5a,5a,5a,5a,5a,5a",options="header"]
|===

| name 		| species | sex 	 | age 	| fixed | legs 	| pounds| weeks
| "Sasha" 	| "cat"	  | "female" | 1 	| false | 4 	| 6.5 	| 3
| "Mittens" | "cat"   | "female" | 2 	| true 	| 4 	| 7.4 	| 1
|===

@lesson-instruction{
Discuss with your group:
- Do you know what each of these attributes measures about an animal?
- How many other @vocab{attributes} could we compute from an animal?

Turn to @printable-exercise{animals-dataset.adoc}. What do you Notice? What do you Wonder?
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

Instead of finding similar _songs_ or similar _images_, suppose we wanted to find similar _animals_.

@lesson-instruction{
Tara saw a movie about an animal she loves, and now she wants to adopt a similar animal. Can you help recommend some possible matches?

Turn to @printable-exercise{matching-animals.adoc}
}


@slidebreak{Investigate}



=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{How were the two ways you measured similarity alike? How were they different?}
@A{The two methods both involved thinking about the same nine images of mountain landscapes.}
@A{The two methods both required honing in on certain characteristics of those photos.}
@A{The second method required me to think about distance and space, while the first method did not.}
@A{In the first method, I needed to compare two images with one another before making a decision.}

== Thinking about AI in Pop Culture

=== Overview

Students consider messages from various media about AI.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

Many characterizations of AI envision technology that *doesn't exist yet*. The futuristic (often dystopian) AI imagined in movies, video games, and books can sometimes interfere with the way that we understand the technology of the present.

Let's consider what sort of AI information we've absorbed just by consuming books, movies, TV, and video games, along with our own personal opinions.

@lesson-instruction{
- With a partner, choose one book / movie / TV show / video game that features AI.
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- With your partner, identify *one* of the messages about AI from the list below that the book / movie / TV show / video game you picked supports.
** AI must be safely controlled at all times.
** AI could lead to social isolation.
** AI can help us understand what it means to be human.
** AI can help advance humanity and scientific discovery.
** AI may pose a threat to humanity.
** AI can perpetuate biases and lead to unfair outcomes.
** Humans are helpless in the face of AI.
- Discuss how the media you picked supports the message.
- Record your thinking on @printable-exercise{ai-in-pop-culture.adoc}.
- Share your reflections with the class.
}
