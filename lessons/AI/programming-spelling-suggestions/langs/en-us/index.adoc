== Introducing Contracts
@objective{contract-parts}
@objective{read-contracts}
@objective{contract-use}
@objective{functions-domain-range}

=== Overview
Building on their understanding of @lesson-link{simple-data-types}, students are introduced to functions in Pyret for working with tables, making visualizations, and creating images. They are also introduced to _contracts_ as an "instruction manual for functions". Contracts are a *major* pedagogical component of the curriculum, and being comfortable reading them is absolutely critical.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@right{@image{images/text-app.png, 250}}

Make sure you've got the @starter-file{spell-checker} open, and your Definitions Area includes `results = alt-words("plation", WORDS-L, 4)`.

Our spell-checker gives us lots of data to play with, in the form of tables of suggested words and their edit distances. We know how to save that data using variable definitions, so that we can refer back to it without having to recalculate the whole table.

Can we do anything _interesting_ with that data?

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@ifslide{@right{@image{images/text-app.png, 250}}}

A spell-checker doesn't give us a table of 500 suggestions. Our cell phones don't tell us the edit distance for the suggestions they make. But just because it doesn't show us the edit distance doesn't mean it's not used! Your cell phone and spell checker uses the edit distance to help pick out the most-likely words to suggestion.

We want to take the output of `alt-words` and _do something_ to it, just as spell checkers and cell phones do.

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Let's review what we've learned about Contracts. Every Contract has three parts:

1. *Name* -- the name of the function, which we type in whenever we want to use it
2. *Domain* -- the type(s) of data we give to the function
3. *Range* -- the type of data the function produces

@lesson-point{
Contracts are the _instruction manual_ for functions.

== Composing with Circles of Evaluation

=== Overview

Students learn to work with more than one function at once, by way of Circles of Evaluation, a visual representation of the underlying structure.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

@lesson-instruction{
- Your cell phone doesn't show you every possible suggestion: it only shows you the top 3-5.
- How do you think the functions you've learned could be used to do the same thing?
- Discuss with the other students at your table or nearby.
}

@teacher{Have students share their ideas}


@slidebreak{Launch}
What if we wanted to sort _and_ trim our tables?

- By looking at the Range, we know that `sort` produces a Table

@hspace{2em} @show{(contract 'sort '((t Table) (col String) (ascending Boolean)) "Table")}

- By looking at the Domain, we know that `first-n-rows` _consumes_ a Table

@hspace{2em} @show{(contract 'first-n-rows '((t Table) (num Number)) "Table")}

_But how could the `sort` and `first-n-rows` functions work together?_

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

One way to organize our thoughts is to diagram what we want to do. *Circles of Evaluation* help us think about what we want to do, without worrying about syntax like quotation marks, parentheses, etc. They let us use all our brains for _thinking_, before we use them for _coding_.

The rules are simple:

(1) Every Circle of Evaluation must have one -- and only one! -- function, written at the top.

(2) The arguments of the function are written left-to-right, in the middle of the Circle.

 * Values like Numbers, String, and Booleans are still written by themselves. It’s only when we want to _use a function_ that we need to draw a nested Circle.

(3) Circles can contain other Circles!

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Suppose we want to see the top 5 results of `alt-words`, sorted by increasing edit-distance.
- Turn to @printable-exercise{composing-coe.adoc} and complete the Notice and Wonder section.
}

@do{

(define trim-exp '(first-n-rows (sort results "edit-distance" true) 5))
}

== Data Visualization

=== Overview
Students continue practicing with Contracts, this time creating data visualizations. They also practice their function composition skills using Circles of Evaluation, combining sorting, trimming, and visualization.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

There were only a few suggestions with an `edit-distance` of 1, but more with distances of 2, 3, and 4. As `edit-distance` increases, the number of suggestions increases, too.

@QandA{
@Q{But does it increase by the same amount each time?}
@A{No! It grows by larger amounts for each `edit-distance`!}
@A{It's almost exponentially larger}

@Q{How could you communicate this growth to someone else, without making them scan the whole table?}
@A{You could count the number of suggestions for each `edit-distance`}
@A{You could make a chart}
}

@vocab{Row accessors} allow us to extract data from specific cells of our table by specifying the row number and column title.

@QandA{
