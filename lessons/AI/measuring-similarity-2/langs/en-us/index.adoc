== Unsupervised vs. Supervised Learning
@objective{ai-supervised-v-unsupervised}

=== Overview
Students consider the limitations of the similarity system they built in @lesson-link{measuring-similarity}, and learn the difference between unsupervised and supervised learning.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

In @lesson-link{measuring-similarity}, you learned that images can be represented using different, machine-generated attributes like `WIDTH`, `LUMINANCE`, `ENTROPY`, etc. You also learned how a @vocab{bag of words model} can be used to turn a single column of strings into multiple columns that count the number of times each word shows up in those strings, giving us a count of `red`, `green`, and `blue` pixels.

Similarity models are also _recommendation systems!_ When you ask Google to find an image matching a particular photo, you are asking it to _recommend_ similar images.

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@right{@image{images/blue-v-green-images.png, 300}}

You also learned that these machine-generated attributes can be used to define _spaces_, in which we can plot those images. And once we see those images as points in space, we can use different measures to approximate similarity.

@QandA{
@Q{What similarity measures did we learn?}
@A{Distance similarity - measures the euclidean distance between two points in any number of dimensions}
@A{Angle similarity - measures the angle between rays drawn from the origin to any two points in dimensions of 2 or more}

@Q{What were some limitations of these measures?}
@A{Distance similarity focused on absolute distance rather than proportional distance. Our little red square was so much smaller than the big one that almost any mountain image with a lot of red pixels was "closer", even if those pixels made up a small portion of the mountain image}
@A{Angle similarity is only useful in 2 or more dimensions, because it's the angle between two points on the same line will always be zero.}
}

=== Investigate

@slidebreak{Investigate}
Unfortunately, the system we've built so far has two major limitations compared to real-world ones. Let's start with the first one:

- *We can only _search by document_*, where we pick an image and find similar ones. Google lets us type in a word like "grass"! Spotify lets us type in a word like "jazz", rather than asking to tell it a specific song.

Remember, our system isn't actually _seeing_ the snow on those mountains. And even if could, it has no idea what the word "snow" means unless we humans tell it!

@slidebreak{Launch}
Machine Learning engineers at Netflix, Spotify, and Google have confronted these same problems:

(1) Computers don't know that you like "Single Ladies", but not "Lemonade"

(2) They don't know that Jack Black is in "Kung-Fu Panda" _and_ "The Minecraft Movie"

(3) They can tell us that two jazz songs are similar, but can't just start a "jazz" station

(4) _In our example_, computers know that our snowy mountain pictures are similar, but has no idea that they both have snow in them.

So...what if *we humans* started telling these model about Jack Black, what songs they like, which songs are jazz and which images have snow?

@slidebreak{Investigate}
There are several different times of Machine Learning, and we're about to cross from one into another:

* Unsupervised Learning - the computer is given a corpus of data and asked to find patterns. There's no "answer key"!
* Supervised Learning - the corpus has been labeled, providing a sort of answer key. The computer has to find patterns that map inputs to outputs, staying as close to the correct answers as possible.

@slidebreak{Investigate}
@lesson-point{AI isn't just one thing: it involves different kinds of learning!}

Image search is just one example of how supervised and unsupervised learning show up in AI. Every example of Machine Learning usually starts with one of these two. There are actually other kinds of machine learning too, but we'll get to that later.

How can we find the "sun-ness" of an image?

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@right{@image{images/blue-v-green-images-centroid.png, 300}}

If every image in our corpus is a point in space, then common ground is literally a point _in the middle_. We have a bunch of images containing the sun, and we want to find the center.

In our 2-dimensional Blue-Green Space, for example, the midpoint halfway between `"grass-sun"` and `"sun2"` defines one kind of "sun-ness"!

(Do you remember learning about mean, median, and mode? In Machine Learning, there are algorithms for finding clusters of points in space that are literally named `k-means`, `k-medians`, and `k-modes` clustering! _Measures of Center are a critical part of modern Machine Learning._)

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
@QandA{
@ifslide{@right{@image{images/centroid.png, 300}}}
@Q{Our unsupervised `angle-similarity` measure did a fine job recommending similar images when given a grassy or snowy image. Why did it struggle to finding images with the sun?}
@A{The sunset image is _really_ different from the others. Any time we search by the `ID` of the other images (0, 6, or 7), we get other recommendations before image 8.}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
Let's talk about the other major limitation of our unsupervised similarity system, compared to real ones:

- We're only recommending matches based on a _single document_: we can't find images, songs, or anything else based on a _collection_ of liked or disliked documents.

How can our @vocab{centroid} implementation solve this problem, too?

@slidebreak{Investigate}
Building @vocab{centroids} is powerful, because now we can access the power of human-labeled information to reason about _groups_. Instead of looking at one image with grass, we can now look at multiple grassy images and talk about _what they have in common: "grass-ness"_.

And we can use that to solve *both* of the limitations of our unsupervised system!

- We can find a centroid for all the images labeled with "sun", and use that to find __un__-labeled images that are close to "sun-ness". Without centroids, we're stuck comparing images to *one picture with a sun*.
- We can find a centroid for all the _liked_ or _disliked_ images, and then recommend __un__-labeled images close to "liked-ness" and far from "disliked-ness". Without centroids, we're stuck comparing images to *one liked*.

@slidebreak{Investigate}
In each case, the steps are the same:

(1) find all the labeled documents (liked, disliked, tagged, etc)

(2) build a centroid

(3) find un-labeled documents that are similar to the centroid using the relevant columns.

- Calculate the centroids for liked and disliked documents
- Calculate the similarities between every document and both centroids
- Recommendation strength is a simple equation:

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Investigate}
You may recall the steps of @vocab{supervised learning} from a @lesson-link{regression-models-multiple, previous lesson}:

- Human-tagged data is recorded, demonstrating the knowledge to be learned
- The machine learning algorithm finds a *function* that generalizes (hopefully well!) from that data to new, unseen data
- The function is applied to new data.

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
@right{@image{images/navlab.png, 200}}
A self-driving car is given a table of inputs (images and depth maps), each row tagged with how a human turned the steering wheel. Then it found a function that would predict the steering wheel angle from new inputs, and used the function by applying it to new roads.

@QandA{
@Q{In our image search example, what is the human-tagged data for the computer?}
@A{The input table tagged with likes or dislikes, or words}

@Q{What algorithm is being used to find images?}
@A{Some kind of angle measure for similarity, followed by sorting}

@Q{Where is the generalization?}
@A{A new, un-tagged image added to database is still being compared to the pre-computed centroids}
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}
@QandA{
@Q{When you tell Spotify that you like a song, what computations happen on Spotify's servers?}
@A{With a new list of "liked songs", Spotify recalculates my "liked" centroid}

@Q{When you tell Spotify that to make a Jazz station, what computations happen on Spotify's servers?}
@A{Spotify computes the centroid for every song *already tagged* with "jazz", then returns those alongside songs that are similar to the centroid}

@Q{When you search for "sun" on Google Images, what computations happen on Google's servers?}
@A{Google computes the centroid for every image *already tagged* with "sun", then returns those alongside images that are similar to the centroid}

@Q{How does Google know to filter out offensive images of extreme violence?}
@A{Google computes the centroid for every image *already tagged* as extremely violent, then filters our images that are similar to the centroid}
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}
Supervised learning is a large part of what makes Machine Learning models feel like they "understand" humans. When we type in a sentence on Google Images, it feels like somehow the search engine _understood_ what we wanted! But in reality, it's another Data Driven Algorithm cranking through oceans of tagged and un-tagged data.

But this comes at a cost: *someone has to tag millions of images!*

@slidebreak{SynthesizeR}
@right{@image{images/CAPTCHA.png}}
In some cases that's US, clicking on images of buses in order to prove we're human. Those images are real images from a database where the similarity wasn't strong enough to be confident about whether they are or aren't a bus. By asking us to tag them, we are helping to train algorithm.

@slidebreak{Synthesize}
But in other cases it's paid contractors, often in developing countries where tech companies can pay them a few dollars (or pennies!) per day to stare at tens of thousands of images and tag them.

Similarity systems are also used to help screen out violent and offensive images, by comparing them to a corpus of images tagged as violent and offensive. But _someone_ had to look at those images to begin with! Seeing tens of thousands of violent or offensive images can have serious impacts on mental health.

@slidebreak{Synthesize}
We rarely think about the human cost of training the Machine Learning models we use every day. When we ask Google to find similar images or Spotify to recommend music based on our Likes and Dislikes, we rarely think about the important role supervised learning has to play in making it all work...and the humans responsible for providing that supervision.
