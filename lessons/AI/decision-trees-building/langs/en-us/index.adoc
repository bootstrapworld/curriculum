== Predicting Categories @duration{25 minutes}

@objective{ai-create-tree}

=== Overview

Students consider technologies that make predictions and recommendations, and then play a game to help them build a frame of reference for diagramming the decision-making process. By the end of this section, students should know:

- What a decision tree is
- That more than one tree can be constructed for a given dataset
- That decision trees can be "good" or "not good" depending on their shape

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

Consider each of the three situations described below:

- You take an online quiz, which guesses your hairstyle.
- Two individuals who work for the same company and earn the same salary apply for a loan by filling out the same online form at the local bank. One of them is granted a loan, but the other is not.
- A news anchor displays a map with some states colored blue and others colored red. She explains that the map's predictions for the upcoming election were developed using historical census data.
- A camera at the bank captures a picture of your face, immediately identifying who you are.

What do all of these examples have in common?

@slidebreak{Launch}

On the surface, these situations may not seem to have a lot in common. But all of these are examples of @vocab{classifiers}, a kind of function that:

@indented{
(1) Consumes some data

(2) Asks questions about that data

(2) Use the answers to produce a predicted outcome from a list of categories
}

@QandA{
@Q{What data do you think is used for *guessing your hairstyle*?}
@A{Viewing history, search history, channel subscriptions, likes, dislikes, google activity, "not interested" feedback}

@Q{What data do you think is used for *approving loan requests*?}
@A{Debt, assets, payment history, number of open accounts, delinquent accounts, income/revenue, expenses, overdrafts in the last 90 days}

@Q{What data do you think is used to *predict election outcomes*?}
@A{Census data including voter race, gender, income level, party affiliation, and age, as well as prior years' results}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

@vocab{Classifiers} are functions that map inputs to a specific set of possible targets.

[.data-table, cols="1a, 2a, 3a", options="header"]
|===
| Example 			    | Input Data _(known)_				      | Categories _(unknown)_
| Hairstyle Quiz	  | Sex, age, race, music, ZIP... 	  | Ponytail vs. Pixie cut vs. Dreadlocks vs. Mohawk...
| Bank Loan 		    | Income, job, age, credit score... | Approved vs. Not-Approved
| Elections 		    | Past voting, recent polls...	 	  | Red vs. Blue
| Facial Recognition| Your face 						            | Every person in America
|===

For example, the hairstyle quiz can probably rule out "ponytail" if the sex of the person taking the quiz is "male".  A classifier's job to make the best guess possible, and it won't always be right! After all, some people with ponytails are male!

@lesson-point{No classifier is perfect. _Some_ are useful.}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

@vocab{Classifiers} use something called a @vocab{Decision Tree}, asking questions in order to narrow down the possible outcome targets until it reaches a single, predicted outcome.

If you've ever played the game "I spy", you've already got some practice building decision trees!

The rules are pretty simple:

- A player comes up with something they is visible to everyone playing
- They come up with a hint, to help narrow the possible answers. For example: "I spy with my little eye...something blue!"
- The other players ask yes-or-no questions to help them guess what it is

@slidebreak{Investigate-RP}

@lesson-instruction{
@right{@image{images/tree-1.png}}
We're going to play a version of "I Spy", called "I Spy - *Classify*!"...

- Everyone will stand a their desks (or front of the room, etc)
- I'm going to choose one student as the target, but keep their identity secret
- One at a time, you'll ask me yes-or-no-questions to figure out the target
- You can ask about _categories:_ For example, "are they wearing glasses?"
    - If I answer "yes", only students wearing glasses stay up, while the others return to their seats
    - If I answer "no", only student *not* wearing glasses stay
- You can ask about _quantities:_ For example, "are they taller than 5'7"?
    - If I answer "yes", only students taller than 5'7" stay up
    - If I answer "no", only students *not* taller than 5'7" stay up
- I'll keep track of our progress on the board
}

@slidebreak{Investigate-RP}

*I Spy*...a student in this class. Who am I thinking of? *Classify!*

@teacher{
@right{@image{images/tree-2.png}}

- Let your students ask questions one at a time. Write each question on the board in the form of a flow-chart, with "yes edges" and "no edges" (lines labeled with "yes" or "no") branching out to the left and right.
- On each edge, write the number of students for whom each answer is "yes" and "no".
- Keep going, until your class has found the target.

@vspace{1ex}

For now, most of the tree will be empty: your students won't need to ask questions about those who already sat down!
}

@slidebreak{Investigate-RP}

Congratulations! You were able to find the target, by asking just a few questions.

@QandA{
@Q{How many questions did it take for you to find the target?}
@Q{Suppose you all just asked "are they named XYZ?" over and over, until we found the target? Would you eventually have found the answer?}
@A{Yes}
@Q{Why didn't we do that?}
@A{It probably would have taken longer! Asking about groups helped us eliminate possible targets, and narrow down our choices faster.}
}

Let's play a few more rounds. This time, however, we can only _add_ to our @vocab{Decision Tree}: for everything else, we need to ask the same questions in the same order.

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{In this game, what were our "targets"?}
@A{Students in the class}

@Q{How many possible targets did we have?}
@A{The number of students in the class}

@Q{If you could start over, how would you change this @vocab{decision tree}?}
@A{This is a big question! Give students some time to discuss}

@Q{How do you know if a question is a good one to start with or not?}
@A{Good questions help us eliminate many of the remaining categories whether the answer is yes _or_ no}

@Q{Suppose our first question was "has glasses?", and all but one student in the class wore glasses. How many questions would it take if I was thinking of the student who wears glasses?}
@A{Just one! If the answer is "yes", we know exactly who it is!}

@Q{Would that be the best question to start with, if we were going to play the game many times?}
@A{No, because if the answer is "no" we're basically still starting at the beginning}

@Q{Suppose almost all of the students in the class were 5'9" or above. Would "taller than 5f 7in?" make a good first question? Why or why not?}
@A{No, because it's not splitting the equally enough}

@Q{When _would_ "taller than 5f 7in"? make a good first question?}
@A{If it divides the class as close to "in half" as possible}
}
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{How does someone use a decision tree to make predictions?}
@A{Ask the question at the root of the tree, then follow the branches to ask additional questions until we get to a category at the end of a path.}
@Q{Suppose we were playing "Classify", but now you have to guess which *country* I'm thinking of. What might be a good starting question?}
@A{Answers will vary, but look for answers that eliminate groups of countries quickly.}
@A{Is it a country in North America?}
@A{Is it a country where people speak Spanish?}
@A{etc...}
@Q{What do we want to be true, about each question we ask?}
@A{We would want it to cleanly split the list of possible targets as evenly as possible, to guarantee eliminating a significant number of options right off the bat.}
}


== Building Decision Trees from a Training Dataset

@objective{ai-create-tree}

=== Overview
Students refine their intuition for building decision trees, discovering how they are constructed from training datasets.

=== Launch

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@right{[.white-bg]#@image{images/tree-3.png}#}
@lesson-point{A @vocab{decision tree} is one way to model the structure hidden in the data.}

We can visualize them as "upside-down trees" that show decisions and their predicted outcomes. The diagram of our "Classify" game is a decision tree. The examples we talked about at the beginning (Guess-the-haircut, Loan approvals, Election predictions, and facial recognition) all use decision trees.

You've learned that some @vocab{decision trees} are better than others. For example, the _frequency of each category_ affects the "best node" ("if everyone has glasses...").

Machine-learning algorithms generate decision trees from training @vocab{datasets}, using the column headers as questions. Each node in the tree splits the dataset, allowing us to make the best prediction we can about some outcome.

@slidebreak{LaunchR}

@right{@image{images/terminology-tree.png, 300}}

- A @vocab{decision node} splits a dataset around the values in one of its columns. The attribute in the column serves as the "question" that is being asked.
- The @vocab{root node} is the very top @vocab{decision node}. It represents an entire dataset.
- @vocab{Splitting} is the process of creating branches and additional nodes corresponding to subsets of a dataset based on values in one column. (We're sticking to splits that only create two branch nodes, but some decisions trees use splits that create more than two.)
- A @vocab{leaf node} is a node that contains the predicted outcome. These outcomes come from a designated column in the original dataset. Leaf nodes have no outgoing branches.
- The sequence of questions from the root node to an outcome is called a path.

@teacher{Discuss the decision tree you made during your "Classify" game to help students identify the root node, decision nodes, leaf nodes, and paths on the tree so far.
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{InvestigateC}

Remember, a @vocab{classifier} is _just a function!_

- It consumes some input and produces some output
- Each input will only ever have one output (no matter how many times we classify `firepaw`, it will always guess the same species!).

@QandA{
Suppose I want my first splitting question to be "does the animal have a tail?"

@Q{How many animals fall in the "yes" side of the tree?}
@A{11}

@Q{How many animals fall in the "no" side of the tree?}
@A{4}

@Q{What does `firepaw["tail"] == true` do?}
@A{It gets the `tail` value from that row, and checks to see if it's `true`}

@Q{What code would I write to check `frisky`?}
@A{`frisky["tail"] == true`}

Let's look at this same classifier function in Pyret, which works for _any_ row in the table...

```
# tail-classifier :: Row -> String
# consumes an animal, and predicts the species
fun tail-classifier(r):

  else:

  end
end
```

You've probably noticed that - just like any other function - `tail-classifier` has a contract!

@QandA{
@Q{What is the *name* of the function?}
@A{`tail-classifier`}

@Q{What is it's *domain*?}
@A{`Row`}

@Q{What is it's *range*?}
@A{String}

@Q{What split decision is it making at the @vocab{root node} of the decision tree?}
@A{It's asking if the row has a tail}

@Q{How many levels deep is this tree?}
@A{1 level deep, because there is only one level of decision node}

@Q{Can you explain why we got the result we did?}

@Q{What do you think we'll get back if we try the classifier on `frisky`? Why?}

@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}

When choosing a split for our root node, our goal is to ask a question that sorts the data into the _neatest, most organized piles that we can_. For example, it would be ideal to get all the cats on one side, and all the dogs on the other. We can't always get a perfect split like this, but we want to get as close as possible.

But how do we know what root will make the best possible split? To know where to split the data, we need to know how the data is _shaped_ for different species. Let's use visualizations to help us explore this, and compare splits based on `pounds` vs. a split based on `tail`...

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
We can use the `image-dot-plot` from @printable-exercise{what-does-the-code-do.adoc}, which made a dot-plot showing the distribution of animals' `pounds`. Where does it make sense to split?

@centered-image{images/dot-plot-pounds.png}

- Splitting on `r["pounds"] < 9` gives us two groups, cats and dogs on both sides
- Splitting on `r["pounds"] < 6` is tempting, because there's a nice gap in the data! But it leaves cats and lizards on both sides of the split.
- Splitting on `r["pounds"] < 3` is much better, with _all tarantulas and snails_ are on one side
