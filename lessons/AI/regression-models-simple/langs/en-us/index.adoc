== Self-Driving Cars

=== Overview
Students are introduced to ALVINN, the world's first self-driving car, as a compelling example of supervised machine learning. They explore a driving simulator, collect their own training data, and discover that predicting steering angle is a regression problem.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{LaunchR}
--
@right{@image{images/self-driving-car.jpg, 300}}

Self-driving cars feel like the future has really arrived! Many people think they are one of the strongest examples of "AI" really _thinking_, instead of just "following an algorithm". After all, how can a computer possibly make decisions about how to drive???

What do _you_ think?
--

@teacher{Give students time to discuss. Remind them about the AI Effect, and how people also felt when the first spell-checkers were developed!}

@slidebreak{Launch}

Self-driving cars work via a lot of inputs (sensors, cameras, etc.) and a lot of outputs (how much to accelerate, brake, and steer). Steering, for example, is incredibly difficult! A car needs to think about:

- Whether the road ahead bends to the left or right, and how sharply
- How far the car is from the center of the lane
- Whether the car is in-line with the lane, or heading towards one side
- Obstacles and traffic lights that could be in the way
- And lots more!

We're going to explore how @vocab{supervised learning} -- regression, specifically -- allows cars to "know where they're going".

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
--
@right{@image{images/navlab.png, 300}}
Meet ALVINN!

ALVINN (short for Autonomous Land Vehicle in a Neural Network) was developed in 1989 by a team of computer scientists at Carnegie Mellon University. ALVINN is the forebear of today's self-driving cars: a retrofitted Army ambulance equipped with sensors, computers, and small motors to turn the wheel, press the pedals, etc. ALVINN had about one-tenth of the processing power of an Apple Watch -- but was able to safely drive on a very limited course at 70 miles per hour by the early 1990s! How did it happen?
--

@slidebreak{Launch}

As with all machine learning, ALVINN required @vocab{training}.

The goal of that training? Learning how to steer.

Here's what ALVINN's training entailed:

(1) A person drove the vehicle while a front-facing video camera recorded the road.

- In the context of @vocab{supervised learning}, the human driver is often referred to as the _supervisor_.
- The supervisor demonstrates the correct behavior.
- The input-output pairs collected during this phase form the @vocab{training corpus}.

(2) 10 times each second, ALVINN recorded...

- a digitized image of the road ahead, along with a distance to the nearest obstacle
- the steering direction chosen by the driver at that moment

@slidebreak{Launch}

(3) ALVINN's training data can be represented as an input-output table, with road images and obstacle distances as inputs and the correct steering angles as outputs.

@indented{
[cols="^.^1,^.^1,^.^1", stripes="none", options="header"]
|===
| `road-image`                      | `obstacle-distance` (ft) | `steering-angle`
| @image{images/road1.png, 100}     |          21              |   0.02º
| @image{images/road2.png, 100}     |          49              |  55.20º
| @image{images/road3.png, 100}     |          37              | -25.43º
|===

@teacher{
Note: These images were selected for illustrative purposes. The 30x32 pixel images produced by ALVINN were of much lower quality!
}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

(4) Using this dataset, ALVINN uses regression to generate a @vocab{function} for predicting the correct steering angle from the road image and obstacle distance.

@lesson-point{
`road-image` and `obstacle-distance` are the @vocab{explanatory variables}. `steering-angle` is the @vocab{response variable}.
}

(5) After training is complete, a human pushes ALVINN's "run" switch and the vehicle begins driving. As ALVINN drives, it continuously evaluates its learned function to produce steering angles — just as the human driver did during training.

@teacher{
If you want your students to see ALVINN in action, now is a good time to view @link{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ntIczNQKfjQ, "this brief two-minute video"}.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

@QandA{
@Q{What information does ALVINN process before producing a steering angle?}
@A{ALVINN processes the image of the road in front of it, along with an obstacle distance, and evaluates its learned function to produce a steering angle.}

@Q{What did ALVINN learn _from_?}
@A{A human driver — the supervisor — who demonstrated correct steering. Those input-output pairs form the training corpus.}

@Q{What is ALVINN actually _computing_ when it decides how to steer?}
@A{It's evaluating a function: given the inputs (road image, obstacle distance), produce a steering angle. The function was learned from the training data.}

@Q{Why is this called _supervised_ learning?}
@A{Because a human provided the correct answers (steering angles) during training, supervising what the machine learned.}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

In discovering the details of ALVINN's training, you may have noticed three key phases:

1. Human-tagged data is recorded, demonstrating the knowledge to be learned
2. A machine learning algorithm finds a *function* that maps from inputs to the correct outputs
3. That function is applied to new, unseen data

@lesson-point{
We're going to do exactly the same thing — training our own model to steer a virtual self-driving car!
}

@A{The `steering-angle` — how far to turn the wheel.}

@Q{What _type_ of value is a steering angle?}
@A{A number (degrees of rotation).}

== Predicting Steering Angle

=== Overview
Students identify candidate explanatory variables from the training corpus, make scatter plots to explore the relationship between each variable and `steering-angle`, and settle on `curve` as the strongest single predictor.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}
Let's use some Data Science to find some evidence about what really _is_ the best explanatory variable for our `steering-angle`.

@QandA{
@Q{What data visualization would be the best to use here?}
@A{Scatter plots}

@Q{Why?}
@A{They show the relationship between two quantitative variables}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

In the @lesson-link{fitting-models} lesson, we learned that a relationship between two variables shows up as a point cloud in a scatter plot, and that we can describe this relationship with a @vocab{model}.

- The @vocab{explanatory variable} (x-axis) is the input we're predicting _from_.
- The @vocab{response variable} (y-axis) is what we're trying to predict.

Our response variable is `steering-angle`. The training data has several columns that could serve as an explanatory variable:

[cols="^1a,^3a", options="header", stripes="none"]
|===
| Column       | What it measures
| `speed`      | How fast the car is moving
| `curve`      | How sharply the road is curving (positive = right, negative = left)
| `skew`       | How far out of alignment the car's heading is with the road
| `offset`     | How far the car is from the center of the lane
|===

@QandA{
@Q{Which of these do _you_ think would be the best predictor of steering angle, and why?}
@A{Answers will vary — this is meant to generate discussion and surface intuitions before the data speaks.}

@Q{What should we look for in our visualizations, to choose the best explanatory variable?}
@A{The strongest-looking relationship (points most-tightly clustered around a line) is the one that is most strongly connected to `steering-angle`}
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}

@lesson-instruction{
- Make sure @starter-file{self-driving} is open with `training-data` loaded.
- Use `scatter-plot` to explore the relationship between `steering-angle` and each of the four candidate variables.
- Complete @printable-exercise{steering-angle-scatter-plots.adoc}, recording your observations for each.
}

@teacher{
Circulate and encourage students to read the scatter plots critically. Push them to notice whether the relationship looks linear, how much scatter there is, and whether there are obvious outliers.
}

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}

@QandA{
@Q{Which variable showed the *strongest* relationship with `steering-angle`?}
@A{`curve` — it has a clear, nearly linear relationship. As the road curves more sharply in one direction, the steering angle increases proportionally.}

@Q{Which variable showed the *weakest* relationship?}
@A{`speed` — there's a lot of scatter with no clear linear pattern.}

@Q{Did any of the relationships surprise you?}
@A{Answers will vary.}
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{Suppose you had only 2 rows of training data — but they fell in a perfectly straight line. Would you trust a model built from those 2 points?}
@A{No — two points can *always* be connected by a line. That doesn't mean the pattern is real or will generalize.}

@Q{How much data do you think you'd need before you could trust a model?}
@A{There's no single answer, but more data collected across a wider variety of conditions makes a model more trustworthy.}

@Q{Is size-of-dataset the only important factor in what makes a good training set?}
@A{No - we also need deriving data on all kinds of roads, from different offsets, etc.}
}

== Linear Regression

=== Overview
Students manually fit a linear model to the `curve` vs. `steering-angle` data, then use Pyret's `lr-plot` to find the optimal fit. They interpret the model's coefficients, use `mr-code` to generate row-consuming predictor functions, and test their predictors using the `drive` function. Finally, they reflect on what it means to have trained a model.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

Recall from @lesson-link{fitting-models} that a model for a linear relationship is a *line of best fit*:

@center{@big{@math{steering\text{-}angle(curve) = (m \times curve) + b}}}

To find the best line, we need to find values of @math{m} (slope) and @math{b} (y-intercept) that minimize the error between the line and the data points.

@lesson-instruction{
- Turn to @printable-exercise{lr-steering-angle.adoc} and complete the first section: *Fitting a Model by Hand*.
- Use your scatter plot from the previous activity to estimate @math{m} and @math{b}.
- Define your `curve-predictor` in @starter-file{self-driving} using your estimates, then use `fit-model` to see how it fits.
}

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
@right{@image{images/steering-v-curve-lr.png, 300}}

Data scientists use a method called @vocab{linear regression} to find the mathematically _optimal_ values of @math{m} and @math{b} — the line that minimizes error as much as possible.

- In Machine Learning, the act of finding this function from data is called @vocab{training}.
- Linear regression finds straight-line patterns between a set of inputs and an already-known target output.
- This form of learning — where we already know the correct output for each input — is called @vocab{supervised learning}.

Pyret has a function to find and visualize this line, and its contract is the same as `scatter-plot`:

@show{(contract "lr-plot" '((table-name Table) (labels String) (xs String) (ys String)) "Image")}

@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}
@lesson-instruction{
- Use `lr-plot` on `training-data` to find the best-fit line for `curve` vs. `steering-angle`.
- Complete the second section of @printable-exercise{lr-steering-angle.adoc}.
- How does Pyret's optimal model compare to the one you fit by hand?
}

@teacher{
Students will notice that `lr-plot` produces a tighter fit than their hand-drawn model. This motivates the idea that the computer can optimize this much better and faster than humans — which is, after all, the point of machine learning.
}

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
@QandA{
@right{@image{images/steering-v-curve-lr.png, 300}}
@Q{What does the slope of our model tell us?}

@Q{Does the y-intercept make sense in the real world? Why or why not?}

@Q{Which variable had the best @math{S}-value?}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate-DN}
@lesson-instruction{
- Let's use `lr-plot` on `training-data` to find the best predictors for the other possible variables!
- Complete the third section of @printable-exercise{lr-steering-angle.adoc}.
}

@teacher{
These template functions are Number-consuming: `curve-predictor(curve)`, `offset-predictor(offset)`, etc. Students are filling in the learned slope and intercept. This sets up the contrast with the Row-consuming version `drive` needs.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Are we ready to use these predictors to drive the car? Pyret has a `drive` function:

@show{(contract "drive" '((predictor ("Row" "Number"))) "Table"
"runs the driving simulator, using predictor to compute the steering angle at each frame")}

Read that Domain _carefully!_ What do you *Notice*?

@lesson-instruction{
- Complete @printable-exercise{lr-steering-angle.adoc}.
- Spend some time thinking about what the error message you get might be telling you!
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
Notice that `drive` takes a *Row-consuming* function — but `curve-predictor` consumes *Numbers*, not Rows!

@QandA{
@Q{Why does `drive` need a Row-consuming function instead of a Number-consuming one?}
@A{Because at each moment, `drive` has access to the entire row of data — not just one column. A Row-consuming function can decide which columns to look at.}

@Q{How can we get a Row-consuming version of `curve-predictor`?}
@A{We need a new Pyret function that generates Row-consuming code from our training data.}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Pyret has exactly the right tool:

@show{(contract "reg-model-code" '((table-name Table) (explanatory List<String>) (response String)) "Code"
"produces code that defines a trained, row-consuming model (copy/paste into the Definitions Area!)")}

For example: `reg-model-code(training-data, [list: "curve"], "steering-angle")`

@lesson-instruction{
- Use `reg-model-code` to generate a row-consuming predictor for each of the four variables.
- Define them as `curve-predictor` (replacing your existing one), `offset-predictor`, `skew-predictor`, and `speed-predictor`.
- Try each one with `drive(...)`. Which model is the best driver?
}

@teacher{
Students will find that `curve-predictor` drives much better than the others. Allow time for them to try all four and compare results before debriefing.
}

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}

@QandA{
@Q{Which predictor drove the best? Did that match your predictions from the scatter plots?}
@A{`curve-predictor` — yes, it had the strongest linear relationship with `steering-angle`.}

@Q{What does the slope of the `curve` predictor tell us about what the model _learned_?}
@A{For every additional unit of curve sharpness, the model predicts a proportional increase in steering angle. Sharper curve = more steering.}

@Q{What does the y-intercept tell us?}
@A{When the road is perfectly straight (`curve = 0`), the model predicts a steering angle near 0 — meaning don't turn. That makes intuitive sense!}
}

=== Common Misconceptions
- *More steering isn't always better.* Students may expect that a predictor trained on "good" data will drive perfectly. Remind them that a model can only generalize from what it's seen — it has no understanding of the road.

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@lesson-point{
Congratulations — you have just *trained a machine learning model!*
}

@QandA{
@Q{What did "training" mean in this context?}
@A{We provided the computer with a dataset of input-output pairs (curve → steering angle), and it found the function that best maps from input to output.}

@Q{Suppose the car encounters a curve steeper than any in the training data. What will the predictor do?}
@A{It will extrapolate from the pattern it learned — which may or may not be accurate. It has no way of knowing it's outside its training range.}

@Q{What if our training data was all collected on dry roads, and the car now drives on ice?}
@A{The model was never trained on those conditions. It will predict as if conditions are normal — which could be very wrong.}

@Q{When an AI system confidently produces a wrong answer because the input is outside its training data, that's sometimes called a _hallucination_. How does what you just built connect to that idea?}
@A{Our predictor will confidently output a steering angle for any input — even situations it's never seen. It doesn't know what it doesn't know.}
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{Could we train a model using more than one explanatory variable?}

@Q{What would a line-of-best-fit look like on a scatter plot with _two_ explanatory variables?}

@A{Would we have one slope and intercept? Two?}
}


@strategy{Computers aren't people!}{

In conversations about AI, computers often get anthropomorphized — given human-like traits when they are not in fact human. This can block students from understanding the mechanics of machine learning.

Avoid saying that the model "knows" how to drive, or that it "understands" the road.

A model's output is always the result of *mathematical computation* — evaluating a learned function on an input. All supervised machine learning relies on data, functions, and computation.
}
