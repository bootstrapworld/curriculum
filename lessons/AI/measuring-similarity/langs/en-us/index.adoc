Computers don't experience the world in the same way we do. 

@lesson-point{Where we see a beautiful mountain scene, a computer only sees a grid of @vocab{pixels} – tiny dots that make up the image.} 

Each pixel has a particular amount of Red, Green, and Blue in it. For our purposes, we'll use a scale of 0-255 to record the amount of each color that makes up a pixel. 

- White is made of every color, so a white pixel would have a value of 255 for Red, Green, _and_ Blue. (255, 255, 255)
- A black pixel, on the other hand, is the _absence_ of color, so it would have zeros for all three colors. (0, 0, 0)
- A red pixel uses all of the possible red and no blue or green (255, 0, 0)
- An orange pixel might use most of the red, some of the green, and no blue (254, 165, 0)

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Computers can look at groups of pixels to compute values. For example:
@right{@image{images/butterfly.png, 100}}

- How blue the image is. 
- *Symmetry* - measures "how similar are the pixels on one side of the image to the pixels on the other side of the image?". 
- *Luminance* measures @vocab{central tendency} - "what's the average brightness of this image?"
- *Entropy* measures @vocab{spread} - "how much variation in brightness is there?"

== Measuring Distance in "Image Space"
@objective{ai-similarity}

=== Overview
Students are introduced to _distance_ as a notion of similarity. This requires them to visualize images in a spatial system - first in one dimension, then in two, then eventually three.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

Pyret has several functions for measuring similarity, and they each work a little differently. The `simple-similarity` function is all-or-nothing: either the images match _precisely_ (1), or they don't (0). 

We want something with more nuance.

@center{@image{images/mountains-grid.png}}

@QandA{
@Q{Which images would you identify as having a lot of grass in them?}
@A{3 and 6}

@Q{Which color column would you expect to have similar values for two grassy images?}
@A{green}
}

- Find the images we identified as grassy on the plot. 
- What do you notice?

@teacher{
The big breakthrough here is recognizing that these columns each represents a _number line_, and every image falls _somewhere on that line_. This allows us to imagine our images existing as points in space, and opens up new ways to measure similarity by thinking about distance between those points.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

We used the `"green"` column to define a number line - a "Green Space" - where every image was plotted according to how many green-dominant pixels it had. And our two grassy images are located near each other.

@QandA{
@Q{How could we quantify how similar images are to each other in this "green space"?}
@A{We could calculate the distance between them.}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

Pyret has a function for computing the `distance-similarity`. It computes the distance between every row in the table and a Row with a given ID and builds a new column with those values.

@show{(contract "distance-similarity" '("Table" "Str" "List<String>") "Table")}

For example, we could evaluate `distance-similarity(decorated-bag, "3", [list: "green"])` to compute how similar each image is to image 3.

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Overall, `distance-similarity` is a huge upgrade compared to `simple-similarity`. But it suffers from two major problems:

- It focuses on _count_, rather than _proportion_: For example, a giant mountain photo has far more red pixels than a tiny red square—and thus appears less 'similar' by this metric—even if red makes up only a tiny fraction of the mountain image.
- It ignores _orientation_: Humans see two "mostly red" images as similar, but the distance formula doesn't. It only calculates the space between points, ignoring where they _actually lie on the graph_.

== Measuring Angles in "Image Space"
@objective{ai-similarity}
@objective{ai-high-dimensions-similarity}

=== Overview
Students are introduced to _angle-measurement_ as a notion of similarity. This reinforces the visualization of images in a spatial system.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}
Let's try another form of similarity, which relies on the idea that "red-dominant" images will all cluster on one axis, and "blue-dominant" places on the other. _Angle similarity_ compares the angles formed by two rays drawn from two points to the origin.

@lesson-instruction{
- Complete the last section on @printable-exercise{from-2-to-3.adoc}
}

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@right{@image{images/3-d-comparison-w-lines.png, 300}}

We can draw lines (called "rays") from the origin to any of our image-points in space. Instead of computing the distance between these points, we can compute the _angle between these lines_. Two points that are identical will have an angle of *0°*, meaning "the greatest possible similarity". Two points that fall on totally different axes (all-blue v. all-red, for example) will have an angle of *90°*, meaning "the least possible similarity."

@slidebreak{Launch}
This solves both of the problems with `distance-similarity`:

- Count v. Proportion: The angle between both of our red squares will be zero, since they both fall exactly on the `red` axis. This correctly identifies that our two red squares are similar.
- Orientation: Angle measure, by definition, describes how closely two rays are oriented.

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@ifslide{@right{@image{images/3-d-comparison-w-lines.png, 300}}}
@QandA{
@Q{Does angle-similarity work in 1-dimension? Could we have used it in our "Green Space"?}
@A{No! One limitation of angle similarity is that the angle will *always be zero* unless we compare documents across multiple dimensions.}

@Q{Are Images the only things we can plot in 3-dimensional space and use angle-similarity for?}
@A{No! We could plot anything with quantitative attributes}
}

@strategy{Isn't this Middle School Math?!}{
Yes, it is!

The same points and angles your students plot and compute in middle-school are sitting at the heart of the machine learning calculations that power so many things in the world today. Even the plagiarism detectors that might be checking their essays are computing angles. So if your students ask “When are we ever going to use this?”, you can tell them, “You already do, all the time.”

If you have older students who have learned some trigonometry, you might want to share that real machine learning systems use the cosine of the angle. There are two reasons for this:

- It’s simpler to compute the cosine directly.
  * In fact, inside Pyret, `angle-similarity` actually computes the cosine _first_, then converts the result into an angle!)
- The angle itself is a somewhat awkward value to work with.
  * The idea that 0° means "most similar" and that 90° (the largest angle) actually means the _least_ similarity can be counter-intuitive.
  * In contrast, the cosine has a nice numeric range for angles between 0° and 90°, going from 1 to 0, which makes it convenient to use in various other mathematical settings. (Specifically, it's used in a process called @link{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient_descent, gradient descent} that is critical to modern AI. (Fun fact: gradient descent is actually just a real-world application of the @link{https://towardsdatascience.com/understanding-the-mathematics-behind-gradient-descent-dde5dc9be06e/, Chain Rule and Power Rule from Calculus}!)

For the purposes of this curriculum, you can safely ignore the difference between angle and cosine similarity! But, for students who are familiar with cosine and curious to explore, all of the starter files in our AI pathway have access to a `cosine-similarity` function.
}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

Angle similarity really helped us improve the machines' "perception" of what makes two images similar. Given one image, we're able to produce much better search results! Thinking back to the notion of search as a *recommendation system*, you could also imagine doing the same with other kinds of data: picking a song and asking Spotify to recommend similar matches!

@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{It's obvious why `angle-similarity` works in two dimensions: you can use a protractor on a piece of paper if you have to! How could you explain why it still works in *three dimensions*?}
@A{All we need is two rays coming from the origin, forming a "V" shape like the bands in a slingshot. No matter how you turn and twist the "V", it will always define a plane on which we can use a protractor.}

@Q{Given this image corpus, which includes our red squares, why couldn't we use angle similarity on a Blue-Green Space?}
@A{Angle similarity requires drawing two rays, so that the angle between them can be measured. But our red squares have zero as their value for both `blue` and `green`, so their coordinates in Blue Green Space would be @math{(0,0)}. It's impossible to draw a line between a point and itself, so there's no way to measure the angle between either of our red squares and any other image.}

@Q{Are there any limitations to using angle measures for similarity, where distance might be better?}
@A{If we're only measuring in 1 dimension, the angle will always be zero}
}

@slidebreak{Synthesize}

There are still a lot of limitations to our system, however:

- We can only _search by document_, where we pick an image and find similar ones. Google lets us type in a word like "grass"! (A music example: Spotify lets us type in a word like "jazz", rather than asking to tell it a specific song)
- We're only recommending matches based on a single document: we can't find images, songs, or anything else based on a _collection_ of liked or disliked documents.
