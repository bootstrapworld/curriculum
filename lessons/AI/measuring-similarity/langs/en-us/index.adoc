=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

Computers don't experience the world in the same way we do. Where we see a beautiful mountain scene, a computer only sees a grid of _pixels_ – tiny dots that make up the image. Each pixel has a particular amount of Red, Green, and Blue in it.

For our purposes, we'll use a scale of 0-255 for each color that makes up a pixel. White is made of every color, so a white pixel would have a value of 255 for Red, Green, _and_ Blue. A black pixel, on the other hand, would have zeros for all three colors.

- A medium, blue pixel would only have _some_ Blue in it, and nothing else: `(0, 0, 150)`
- A light purple pixel might have have a mix of Red and Blue, and no Green at all: `(255, 0, 255)`

@slidebreak{Investigate}

== Simple Similarity

=== Overview
Students explore a corpus of mountain images, and practice making image scatter plots to see how these images are distributed across different 2-dimensional spaces.


=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

Let's explore the rest of this @starter-file{ai-image}!

- Line 13-22 define each of our mountain images
- These images (and our red squares!) are used in the `image` corpus, which is defined on the next few lines
- Processing images uses a lot of computing power, so we _scale_ the large, mountain images in our corpus to be half their original size
- Under the table, we define a new value called `decorated`. What is it?


@slidebreak{Launch}
It would be nice to take _color_ into account, and measures these images according to how much Red, Green, or Blue they have. At the moment, however, that information is locked inside the `"COLOR-NAMES"` string. *How could we calculate the amount of each color that's in each image?*


@slidebreak{Launch}
Pyret has a special function that takes in a Table and the name of a column, and _replaces that column_ with a bunch of columns that count the number of times each word appears in the corpus.

Evaluate `decorated-bag` in the Interactions Area, to see what this looks like.

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
@right{@image{images/bag-of-colors.png, 150}}
The `add-bag-cols` function counted the number of reds, greens and blues in `COLOR-NAMES`!

We don't really care about the _order_ of those words - we just want to know how many pixels are red-dominant, green-dominant, or blue-dominant!

Without the order of the words, it's as if we just put all the words into a bag and shook them up! The information about _frequency_ of each word is still there, but we've lost the _order_. Machine Learning Engineers call this a @vocab{bag-of-words model}.

@slidebreak{Launch}
Let's review where we are:

- We've created a @vocab{model} called `decorated-bag` from our image corpus, which captures certain information -- luminance, vertical symmetry, etc.
- We've left the original images in the `DOC` column for us humans to see, but to a computer they don't matter at all!

The $64,000 question is: can we do something like Google Image Search, using only the machine-generated attributes in our table? For example, could we look for images similar to one of our snow-covered mountains (like `"4"`) and have the computer produce the other (`"5"`)?

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

Pyret has several functions for measuring similarity, and they each work a little differently. Let's start with the simplest:

@indented{@show{(contract "simple-similarity" '("Table" "String" "List<String>") "Table")}}

Pyret's `simple-similarity` function _compares every row in the table_ to the Row with the given ID, checking to see if the specified columns are *identical*. If they are, the similarity value is one. If not, the similarity value is zero. It produces a Table, sorted from "most similar" to "least similar", with an extra column of zeroes and ones listing the similarity score.


For example, we could check which images have the same `SYMMETRY-V` as our little red square (whose ID is `"9"`):

@show{(code '(simple-similarity decorated-bag "9" (list "SYMMETRY-V")))}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{What did `simple-similarity` produce?}
@A{Our decorated table again, but now there's another column called `similarity`}

@Q{Was the little red square similar to itself?}
@A{Of course!}

@Q{Was the big red square similar to the little one? Why or why not?}
@A{Yes! They have exactly the same vertical symmetry}
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Let's try comparing _both_ symmetry columns:

@show{(code '(simple-similarity decorated-bag "9" (list "SYMMETRY-V" "SYMMETRY-H")))}

@QandA{
@Q{What do you think we'll get back, and why?}
@A{The same table, because both squares are perfectly symmetric in both ways}

@Q{Could we get the same result using any of our other columns?}
@A{Yes - the `red` column would also work}

@Q{Would we get the same result for every possible columns?}
@A{No - the `WIDTH` and `HEIGHT` are different}
}

Try it yourself!

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{Is `simple-similarity` a _useful_ way to measure similarity?}
@A{Not at all.}

@Q{Why or why not?}
@A{Two images can be similar even if they aren't *identical*!}

@Q{What would make a measure of similarity more useful?}
@A{We should at least be able to tell the difference between an image that comes close and another that's totally different.}

@Q{Could we use `simple-similarity` to compare our snowy mountain images? Why or why not?}
@A{No - they are very similar, but they are not identical!}
}

== Distance Similarity

=== Overview
Students are introduced to _distance_ as a notion of similarity. This requires them to visualize images in a spatial system - first in one dimension, then in two.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}
The `simple-similarity` function is all-or-nothing: either the images match _precisely_ (1), or they don't (0). We want something with more nuance, which can tell that one grassy image is a lot closer to the other one than it is to a red square! A simple 1-and-0 score isn't good enough.

What else could we do?

@slidebreak{Launch}
One option is to think of these columns as _number lines_, and plot the images on those lines. The distance between the images tells us how similar (or different) they are.

@lesson-instruction{
Complete the first section on @printable-exercise{plotting-images.adoc}
}

@slidebreak{Launch}
Pyret has a function for computing the `distance-similarity`. Just like `simple-similarity`, it compares every row in the table to a Row with a given ID.

@hspace{1em}@show{(contract "distance-similarity" '("Table" "String" "List<String>") "Table")}

This is definitely a big improvement! By plotting the images in "Green Space", we can clearly see that one grassy image is the _most similar_ to the other one.

But comparing images based only on one property (how many green pixels they have) is still pretty limited. Can we compare images based on _more than one?_

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
@right{@image{images/projection.webp, 300}}

This image represents a _projection_ of a 3D object into two, 2-dimensional planes. You can see that one shadow looks like a square and the other looks like a circle. Both are showing us a part of the story, and both are correct about the part they show. But the _real_ shape is 3-dimensional (3D).

*The same is true for our dataset!*

Our images are described by 3 different colors (Red, Blue, and Green), so our "Color Space" has three dimensions! Like the shadows in this image, our two scatter plots in "Blue-v-Green Space" and "Blue-v-Red Space" are just _projections_ of 3D points onto the Z-X and Z-Y planes.

The distance formula works in 3 dimensions too! In fact, it works in _any number of dimensions_ we want -- we just can't see more than 3 with our eyes. But *the math holds true*, and we can use `distance-similarity` with any number of attributes we want.

@lesson-instruction{

- Try it again with ID `"5"`, to see how well it works at finding the similarity between images with *snow*.
- Try it again with ID `"10"`, to see how well it works at finding the similarity between images with *red squares*.
- Try it again with ID `"6"`, to see how well it works at finding the similarity between images with *sun*.
- Try adding _other_ columns! Can you come up with a higher-dimensional space that works for all four groups?
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Overall, `distance-similarity` is a huge upgrade compared to `simple-similarity`. But it suffers from two major problems:

- It focuses on _count_, rather than _proportion_: our small red square and big red square are obviously similar, but distance only counts the number of red pixels. Our larger mountain images have more red pixels than the tiny square, even if only a small percentage of the overall image is red!
-
- It ignores _orientation_: As humans, we know that two images that are "mostly red" are a lot more similar that two images that are different colors! But the distance formula doesn't care if two images are dominated by the same color or not -- when calculating the distance _between_ the points it doesn't consider _where those points are on the graph._

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
@right{@image{images/3-d-comparison-w-lines.png, 300}}

Let's try another form of similarity, which relies on the idea that "red-dominant" images will all cluster on one axis, and "blue-dominant" places on the other. _Angle similarity_ compares the angles formed by two rays drawn from two points to the origin.

Of course, Pyret has a function for angle similarity, too!

@hspace{1em}@show{(contract "angle-similarity" '("Table" "String" "List<String>") "Table")}

This function _compares every row in the table_ to the Row with the given ID, calculating the angle-similarity between the points specified by the columns.

@strategy{Isn't this Middle School Math?!}{

Yes, it is!

The same points and angles your students plot and compute in middle-school are sitting at the heart of the machine learning calculations that power so many things in the world today. Even the plagiarism detectors that might be checking their essays are computing angles. So if your students ask “When are we ever going to use this?”, you can tell them, “You already do, all the time.”

If you have older students who have learned some trigonometry, you might want to share that real machine learning systems use the cosine of the angle. There are two reasons for this:

- It’s simpler to compute the cosine directly.
  * In fact, inside Pyret, `angle-similarity` actually computes the cosine _first_, then converts the result into an angle!)
- The angle itself is a somewhat awkward value to work with.
  * The idea that 0° means "most similar" and that 90° (the largest angle) actually means the _least_ similarity can be counter-intuitive.
  * In contrast, the cosine has a nice numeric range for angles between 0° and 90°, going from 1 to 0, which makes it convenient to use in various other mathematical settings. (Specifically, it's used in a process called @link{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient_descent, gradient descent} that is critical to modern AI. (Fun fact: gradient descent is actually just a real-world application of the @link{https://towardsdatascience.com/understanding-the-mathematics-behind-gradient-descent-dde5dc9be06e/, Chain Rule and Power Rule from Calculus}!)

For the purposes of this curriculum, you can safely ignore the difference between angle and cosine similarity! But, for students who are familiar with cosine and curious to explore, all of the starter files in our AI pathway have access to a `cosine-similarity` function.
}
