= Measuring Similarity

@description{
Students consider how arbitrary documents (in this case, songs and images) can be represented as points in space, and explore different ways to measure the "distance" between those documents using a spatial representation.
}

== Categories, Attributes, and Dimensions

=== Overview

Students explore the idea of _dimensionality_ -- that virtually any category of thing can be defined as having a set of attributes (e.g. - Fast Food chains can be defined by _price_ and _quality_), and any member of that category can be graphed as a point in space (e.g. - plotting fast food restaurants on a _price-quality_ graph).

Suppose someone is a die-hard fan of a popular fast-food chain from the west coast...In-N-Out!

Unfortunately, In-N-Out isn't available everywhere, and this person is going to have to settle for one of the places in our table. Based on this data, which of the five chains in our table is _most similar_ to In-N-Out, and why?

@teacher{Make sure you have students explain their thinking!}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
If we're just comparing based on Price, that's easy: plot the restaurants' prices on a number line, and find the one that's closes to In-N-Out.

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@right{@image{images/2-d-comparison.png, 400}}
But what if we want to compare based on Price _and_ Fry Quality? For this, we'll need a @vocab{scatter plot}!

Let's take a look at this @online-exercise{https://www.desmos.com/3d/lhqra70cje, Fast Food Ratings} graph.

- At the top of the expression panel on the left, you'll see a data table for our restaurants.
- We have columns for Price, Fry Quality, Burger Quality, and "restaurant number" (e.g., 1 = McDonalds).
- This 2-dimensional scatter plot shows the relationship between Price (Z) and Fry Quality (X).
- We can "turn on" the folder for "In-N-Out", to see where it falls on the graph.
- We want to find the blue dot that's most similar to the green one.
- *How do we measure "similarity" in 2 dimensions?*
--

@slidebreak{Investigate}
@teacher{Turn on the folder "Lines and Angle Calculation". A line will appear, pointing from the origin to the point for In-N-Out.}

@QandA{
By dropping lines from each point to the origin, we can *measure the angle between them!*
@right{@image{images/2-d-comparison-w-lines.png, 400}}
@Q{What is would be the _largest possible angle_ between any two points on this graph?}
@A{90°: a fictional restaurant with $0 in price and the best fries, and another one with a zero for fries and a high price.}

@Q{What is would be the _smallest possible angle_ between any two points on this graph?}
@A{0°: two restaurants with the exact same score will result in two lines going to the same point from the origin.}
}

@teacher{Adjust the slider for @math{r_n}, showing the angle @math{θ} between In-N-Out and the other restaurants.}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
We can repeat the same process with our Burger v. Price scatter plot!

@teacher{
- Refresh the page to reset everything
- Turn off the folder for "Fries v. Price" and turn *on* the folder "Burgers v. Price"
- Click on the "X" label on bottom axis, and slowly drag it to the left.
- This will rotate the 3D space, until you have a "flat" z-y plane with blue dots on it
- Turn on "In-N-Out" and "Lines and Angle Calculation", and find the closest blue dot
}

In every case, we draw lines from our points to the origin and then compute the _angle-distance_ between them. But what if we want to compare Price, Fry Quality, _and_ Burger Quality? How can we compare all three?

@teacher{Solicit student responses!}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
--
@right{@image{images/projection.webp, 300}}

This image represents a _projection_ of a 3D object into two, 2-dimensional planes. You can see that one shadow looks like a square and the other looks like a circle. Both are showing us a part of the story, and both are correct about the part they show. But the _real_ shape is 3-dimensional (3D).

The same is true for our dataset! Our restaurants are described by 3 different attributes, so our "restaurant space" really has three dimensions! Like the shadows in this image, our two scatter plots are just _projections_ of 3D points onto the Z-X and Z-Y planes.
--

@slidebreak{InvestigateR}
--
@right{@image{images/3-d-comparison-w-lines.png, 400}}

- Turn *on* "Fries v. Price" again, as well as "Fries v. Burgers v. Price".
- Rotate the 3D space to match the projection image, so you can see how the 3D plot is "projected" onto the planes to create our 2D scatter plots.
- Now turn *off* the 2d scatter plots ("Fries v. Price" and "Burger v. Price"), and turn *on* our "Lines and Angle Calculation"

Even in three dimensions, we can draw our lines and measure the angle distance!
