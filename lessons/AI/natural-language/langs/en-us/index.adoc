== Plotting Essays in Space
@objective{ai-bag-of-words}
@objective{ai-training-lossy}

=== Overview
Students review their progression from plotting numerical data to plotting image data, then consider the challenge of plotting human-written text. They apply the bag of words model from @lesson-link{measuring-similarity-2}, and confront the extra challenges that natural language presents.

=== Launch

Today we are going to explore how Machine Learning Models work with @vocab{natural language} - languages that humans use - starting with some texts written in English.

@slidebreak{LaunchR}
Let's think back to our discussion of @lesson-link{measuring-similarity, similarity}. 

@lesson-point{
We can *plot documents in space* and use *geometry* to calculate something approximating "similarity".
}

@right{@image{images/3-d-comparison-w-lines.png, 300}}

- A table with @math{n} numeric attributes (e.g., number of green-dominant pixels) can be graphed as points in @math{n}-dimensional space
- The location of these points in space can be used to compute different measures of similarity (equality, distance similarity, angle similarity, etc.)
- Machine Learning Engineers typically use angle measure, because it more closely reflects human intuition for things clustered on an axis being more similar to each other than things clustered on different axes.

@slidebreak{Launch}

@lesson-point{If the input data _isn't_ numeric, we can compute numbers anyway!}

- In our @opt-starter-file{ai-image}, we generated numeric attributes like `SYMMETRY`, `LUMINANCE`, and `ENTROPY`
- We also generated very long Strings of `COLOR-NAMES`, listing each of the dominant primary colors in pixel order.
- We used a @vocab{bag of words model} to turn our ordered string of colors into an _un-ordered_ count of the number of `red`-, `green`-, and `blue`-dominant pixels, allowing us to plot images in a 3-dimensional "color-space"

== Data Normalization
@objective{ai-data-normalization}

=== Overview
Students explore the importance of @vocab{data normalization}, when we organize data to follow a standard pattern.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}

Now that we're using a @vocab{bag of words model} for text that's more complex than our long lists of `red`, `blue`, and `green` pixels, we're ending up with a lot of columns, many of which don't contain much useful information.

@lesson-instruction{
- Let's take a moment to consider *what humans know about text that machines don't*.
- Complete the first section of @printable-exercise{normalizing-text.adoc}.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

@QandA{
@Q{Why didn't Pyret identify the same number of `badger` references in the text as you did?}
@A{Our data is "messy":
  * Punctuation: `badger` and `badger,` aren’t different words
  * Capitalization: `By` and `by` aren’t different words
  }

@Q{What are some words that you would suggest Pyret ignore when looking for similarity between documents?}
@A{is, a, in, to, the, and, of, by, with, as...}

@Q{What ideas do you have for how to improve Pyret's ability to recognize words that are the same as the same?}
@A{Listen to student word choices as they try to articulate this big idea! Then introduce vocabulary.} 
@A{Cleaning the data before we make our bag of words model:
  * remove capitalization
  * remove punctuation from the end of words
  * remove words that appear in every document
  }
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

Our data is "messy".

Inconsistent *capitalization* and *punctuation* prevent the computer from recognizing words as the same. As a result:

. Our counts for these word are off, so we're not seeing how common they really are.
. We're creating extra, meaningless dimensions in which text gets compared!

The presence of @vocab{stop words} might lead the computer to falsely compute texts as similar.

. It creates many dimensions in which the essays are close together.
. Those dimensions raise the similarity score between two essays simply because they're both English! 

Addressing this "messiness" is called @vocab{data normalization}.

@slidebreak{Investigate}

Machine Learning Engineers and Data Scientists care a lot about @vocab{normalizing} their data in order to cut out the irrelevant noise and focus on what matters. We've provided three normalization functions for you to experiment with:

@hspace{1em}@show{(contract "remove-punct" '("String") "String")}

@hspace{1em}@show{(contract "remove-stops" '("String") "String")}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Complete the second section of @printable-exercise{normalizing-text.adoc} to explore what each of these functions does.

@QandA{
@Q{What do you Notice? What do you Wonder?}
@A{Answers will vary, but may include:
  * The text gets much shorter.
  * The text doesn't totally make sense after its normalized
  * The `remove-punct` function is imperfect because `badger's` becomes `badgers`, which will still end up in its own column
  * We don't have a function for removing plurals.
  } 
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-instruction{
- Let's see whether Pyret can identify which document is most similar to `?`
- Complete the last section on @printable-exercise{normalizing-text.adoc}.
}

@slidebreak{Investigate}

@QandA{
@Q{Before we normalized our data, did Pyret compute `elephant` as the most similar text to `mystery`?}
@A{Yes.}

@Q{Before we normalized our data, did Pyret compute any other texts to be somewhat similar to `mystery`?}

@Q{How did that compare to the similarity computations after we normalized our data?}

@Q{Explain why normalizing the data changed the results?}
@A{Removing garbage data and common words allowed us to focus our comparison on only the words that matter, and count them properly.
}

@Q{Which bag of words model had more dimensions, the one for `computed` or `norm-computed`?}
@A{`computed`. Our `norm-computed` model should have far fewer columns!}

@Q{Would you expect a bag of words model built with 1000 columns or 200 columns to be better?}
@A{It depends! The number of columns of data available is not a good way to determine how good a bag of words model will be!}
}

=== Synthesize

@QandA{
@Q{What does it mean to normalize text?}
@A{To standardize it to reduce redundancies and noise.}
@A{Goal 1: make sure that each occurrence of a word ends up being counted as the same word.}
@A{Goal 2: remove @vocab{stopwords} so they don't lead texts to falsely be computed as similar.}

@Q{What might it look like to normalize data involving addresses?}
@A{standardize how names are written so we don't end up with separate entries for Drive, Dr. and Dr}

@Q{What might it look like to normalize data about grade levels?}
@A{standardize so that eighth, 8th and 8 aren't all recorded as separate}

@Q{What might it look like to normalize data about phone numbers?}
@A{standardize the number of digits and formatting expected}
@A{do we want to start with country code?}
@A{do we want to use spaces or hyphens or none?}

@Q{What might it look like to normalize data about people's height?}
@A{standardize units: do we want to go with 5'8" or 68" or 1.72m or 172cm?}
}

@teacher{
Words are just one kind of token that can be used by a language and processed by a @vocab{bag of words model}. Musical tokens might be notes. Pyret tokens could be simple values (e.g. Number, String, Boolean) or syntax (e.g. `fun`, `end`, `if-else`). In a chess game the tokens might be moves in the game.
}

== Detecting Plagiarism
@objective{ai-plagiarism-detector-ethics}
=== Overview
Armed with their similarity detection, students explore the how that algorithm can be used to detect plagiarism. Now that they know how it works, they consider limitations of the algorithm and when it might not be ethical for teachers to use it.

=== Launch
@slidebreak{Launch}
A @vocab{training corpus} is a collection of data used to train AI/ML @vocab{models}, enabling them to learn patterns and make predictions.

For a plagiarism checker, the corpus is the set of documents previously fed into the checker.

@QandA{
@Q{What sorts of documents make up the training corpus of an _effective_ plagiarism detector? List as many as you can.}
@A{The corpus would likely include:
  * essays written and submitted by students currently in the class
  * essays written and submitted by students previously in the class
  * Wikipedia articles
  * articles on relevant topics that are available on the internet, etc.}
}

Suppose a teacher builds a plagiarism detector for short animal essays. She turns paragraphs for ten different animals into bags of words and adds them to the model. This setup is done only _once_, and can be reused for any student submission.

The @vocab{training} phase is where the enormous corpus of essays, wikipedia articles, etc is converted to a bag-of-words for each document, and stored. 

*Our `bags` table is our model!*

=== Investigate
@slidebreak{Investigate}

@lesson-point{Once a @vocab{model} is @vocab{trained}, the corpus can be queried as many times as we want without having to repeat any of the work done during training!}

@slidebreak{Investigate}
To catch plagiarism across _any_ essay, a teacher needs a model trained on a _massive_ document collection. Processing this large corpus creates a complex, high-dimensional space where each unique word adds a new dimension.

@QandA{
@Q{If a teacher feeds twenty 500-word student essays into a plagiarism detector to use as a training corpus, about how many dimensions will the resulting model have?}

@A{Students should provide a wide range of estimates.}
@A{The largest possible estimate would be 10,000 dimensions (20 essays multiplied by 500 words) --but it is not a good estimate, because we commonly repeat and reuse words like "the", "and", "a", and so on.}

@A{Before making an estimate, students might have clarifying questions, like:
  * Did all of the students write about the same topic?
  * How sophisticated is the student writing?
  * Did all students actually write 500 words?
}

@A{A reasonable prediction would probably be that there would be at least a few hundred dimensions in the model.}
}

@lesson-instruction{
Complete @printable-exercise{matching-human-judgment.adoc}.}

=== Synthesize
@slidebreak{Synthesize}

@QandA{
@Q{What strategies did you use to complete the matching activity? Were any of the scenarios especially challenging to match?}

- In Class A, 3rd graders are asked to write 1-paragraph essays about zebras
- In Class B, high school freshmen are asked to write a 3-page paper about their favorite TV show

@Q{Is it *equally ethical* for the teachers in both classes to use plagiarism detectors with their students' work? Why or why not?}
@A{Definitely not!}
@A{3rd graders have smaller vocabularies, if all the essays are about zebras the number of unique words will be small (they'll all use "Zebra", "Stripes", and "Africa", for example). And being just one paragraph long, there's not a ton of room for the frequency of words to differ! It's very likely there will be false positives in Class A, and a plagiarism detector is just not appropriate.}
}
