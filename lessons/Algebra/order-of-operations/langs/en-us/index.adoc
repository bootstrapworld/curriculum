== Order of Operations

=== Overview
Students are given a challenging expression that exposes common misconceptions about Order of Operations. The goal is to demonstrate that a brittle, fixed notion of Order of Operations is insufficient, and lead students to a deeper understanding of Order of Operations as a _grammatical_ device, rather than a computational one. The Circles of Evaluation can be thought of as "sentence diagramming for arithmetic".

@ifnotslide{
@strategy{Circles of Evaluation}{

@right{@image{images/pemdas.png, 200}} Students are often introduced to the Order of Operations by way of this pyramid model, or mnemonics like @link{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations#Mnemonics, "PEMDAS, GEMDAS, or BODMAS"} as a playbook for _sequencing computations_. This is a brittle and limited (mis)understanding of the core concept: the Order of Operations is actually a playbook for sequencing _groupings_!

Consider the expression @math{ 0 \times (3x^3 \div -4(\sqrt{14} - \frac{4}{3})) }:

- *Sequence of computations* -- tells us that we need to compute the value inside the parentheses first, before multiplying by zero.
- *Sequence of groupings* -- tells us that everything inside the parentheses _will eventually be multiplied by zero_. Students can use the zero property of multiplication to immediately compute the right answer, without ever having to deal with fractions, square roots, or distribution!

The Circles of Evaluation are a pedagogical tool that places the focus on the _structure_ of mathematical expressions. Students apply the same rules they've already learned (PEMDAS, GEMDAS, etc) to **making the groupings in expressions visible _before doing any computation_**.

Focusing on the structure of an expression allows students to be far more flexible and creative in their solving process, seeing expressions as puzzles to be simplified and solved in different ways, rather than a rote series of computations! And when a student makes a mistake, revealing their thinking through these diagrams allows teachers to notice and address misconceptions.

Circles of Evaluation also serve as a _bridge representation_, which naturally connects to function composition and converting arithmetic into code.
}
}

*The rules for drawing Circles of Evaluation are simple:*

1) Every Circle must have one -- and only one! -- @ifproglang{pyret}{operator or} function, written at the top.

2) The inputs @ifproglang{wescheme}{to the function} are written left-to-right, inside the Circle.

Now that we've seen how Circles of Evaluation connect to expressions, it's time to apply what we know about the Order of Operations to diagram expressions using the Circles of Evaluation.

Let's start with @big{@math{8^2 \div 4 - 5 \times (3 + 9)}}, using an "inside-out" strategy.

First we would parse the groupings formed by parentheses and other grouping symbols.

@big{@math{8^2 \div 4 - 5 \times}} @show{(coe '(+ 3 9))} 

Then we would parse groupings formed by exponents (and radicals like square roots).

@show{(coe '(sqr 8))} @big{@math{ \div 4 - 5 \times}} @show{(coe '(+ 3 9))} 

@teacher{We've used @proglang's dedicated squaring function here, but students could also use the `expt` function, which takes in two numbers.}

Then we would parse groupings formed by multiplication and division, working our way from left to right.

[.oooCOE]
@show{(coe '(/ (sqr 8) 4))} @big{@math{ - }} @show{(coe '(* 5(+ 3 9)))} 

Then we would parse groupings formed by addition and subtraction, working our way from left to right.

[.oooCOE]
@show{(coe '(- (/ (sqr 8) 4) (* 5(+ 3 9)) ))}

@lesson-instruction{
When converting a Circle of Evaluation to code, it's useful to imagine a "spider" crawling through the circle.
}

@slidebreak{Launch}

@lesson-instruction{
@ifproglang{wescheme}{
- The first thing the spider does is enter the circle by crossing over a curved line (an open parenthesis!).
- She then visits the operation -- also called the _function_ -- at the top.
- After that, she crawls from left to right, visiting each of the inputs to the function.
- Finally, she has to leave the circle by crossing another curved line (a close parenthesis).
}

@ifproglang{pyret}{
- The first thing the spider does is enter the circle by crossing over a curved line (an open parenthesis!).@ifnotslide{ +
}@ifslide{ }For Circles of Evaluation with _operators_ (addition, subtraction, etc.):

  * the spider visits the number on the left
  * next she visits the operation at the top of the circle
  * then she visits the number on the right

- Finally, she has to leave the circle by crossing another curved line (a close parenthesis).
}
}

=== Common Misconceptions

@ifproglang{pyret}{
As in math, there are some cases where the outermost parentheses can be removed in Pyret:

- @math{(1 + 2)} can be safely written as @math{1 + 2}, and the same goes for the Pyret code
- @math{(1 * 2) * 3)} can be safely written as @math{1 * 2 * 3}, and the same goes for the Pyret code

You will likely see code written using this "shortcut", but it's always better to at least start with the parentheses to make sure your math/code is _correct_ before taking them out. It is never wrong to include them!
}

@ifproglang{wescheme}{
@teacher{
Eagle-eyed students may notice that WeScheme refers to the operator as a _function_, and ask why it isn't calling them operators. That's because *operators _are_ functions*, and the only reason we use different _vocabulary_ is to highlight the different mathematical _grammar_ (operators go between their arguments, while functions come first). In WeScheme, the notation is the same and so is the vocabulary. This has the added advantage of helping students see the connection between operators and functions.
}
}
